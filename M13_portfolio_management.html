Portfolios & the art
of allocation.

One good investment is a stock pick. A collection of them, deliberately combined so their risks partly cancel, is a portfolio — and it's a fundamentally different, more powerful thing. This is where diversification stops being a slogan and becomes mathematics.

Section 01Why a portfolio > a pile of picks

A portfolio is your total collection of investments treated as one system. The key insight — and it's genuinely profound — is that a portfolio's risk is not the average of its parts' risks. Combine assets that don't move identically and some of their ups and downs cancel out, so the whole is less volatile than its pieces. You get a smoother ride for the same average return. That partial cancellation is the entire reason portfolios exist.

★ The famous line

Diversification is often called "the only free lunch in finance" — the one way to reduce risk without reducing expected return. Everywhere else, less risk means less return. Here, by combining imperfectly-correlated assets, you lower risk for free. It's the closest thing finance has to a law of physics you can exploit.

Section 02Diversification & correlation

The magic ingredient is correlation — how two assets move relative to each other, measured from +1 to −1:

  • +1 — they move perfectly together. Combining them gives no diversification benefit.
  • 0 — they move independently. Significant risk reduction.
  • −1 — they move perfectly opposite. In theory, risk can be eliminated entirely.

Real assets sit in between. Stocks and bonds are often weakly or negatively correlated (when stocks fall in a scare, money flees to bonds), which is exactly why the classic stock/bond portfolio works so well. Gold (M11) earns its place the same way — low correlation, not high return.

⌘ Engineer's note

This is signal-and-noise again. If two noisy signals are independent, averaging them reduces the noise (variance) without reducing the signal (return) — the same reason you average independent measurements to improve precision. Diversification is literally noise-averaging across assets. And the catch is identical: if the "independent" sources share a hidden common failure mode, the averaging fails right when you need it — which is why correlations spike toward 1 in a crash.

Section 03The risk-return trade-off, quantified

To do this properly we need to measure risk with a number. The standard one is volatility — the standard deviation of returns. A high standard deviation means returns swing widely (risky); a low one means they're stable. Expected return is the average you anticipate. Every asset and every portfolio becomes a point on a map of (risk, return) — exactly the map you met in M11.

Portfolio return = wA·rA + wB·rB return is a simple weighted average...
Portfolio risk < wA·σA + wB·σB (when correlation < 1) ...but risk is LESS than the weighted average — that's the free lunch

Return blends linearly; risk does not. That gap between them — risk coming in below the simple average — is the diversification benefit made numerical.

Section 04The efficient frontier

If you plot every possible mix of your assets on the risk-return map, the points trace a curve. The upper edge of that curve is the efficient frontier — the set of portfolios giving the maximum return for each level of risk. Any portfolio below the frontier is "inefficient": you could get more return for the same risk, so you'd never rationally hold it.

This is the core of Modern Portfolio Theory (Harry Markowitz, 1952 — a Nobel Prize), the foundation of M21's CFA curriculum. The practical takeaway is simple even if the math is deep: don't pick assets in isolation; pick the combination that sits on the frontier.

▸ The counter-intuitive bend

Look closely at the curve's left edge: a portfolio of, say, 20–30% stocks can be less risky than 100% bonds, while earning more. Adding a riskier asset reduced total risk. That's not a trick — it's correlation at work, and it's the single most useful, least intuitive fact in portfolio construction. A pure-bond investor is often taking more risk than they need to for their return.

Section 06The Sharpe ratio — return per unit of risk

How do you compare portfolios with different risks and returns on one scale? The Sharpe ratio: excess return (above the risk-free rate) per unit of volatility.

Sharpe = (Return − Risk-free rate) ÷ Volatilityreward-to-risk — higher is better

A Sharpe of 1.0 is good; below 0.5 is mediocre; above 2.0 is exceptional (and often too good to be true). It lets you ask the right question — not "which has the highest return?" but "which delivers the most return for the risk taken?" The best portfolio on the efficient frontier is the one with the highest Sharpe ratio.

Section 07Asset allocation — the big decision

Here's a finding that surprises people: studies suggest your asset allocation — the high-level split between stocks, bonds, gold, cash, etc. — explains the large majority of your portfolio's return variability, far more than which specific stocks you pick. The big lever isn't "which stock?"; it's "what mix of asset classes?"

Allocation flows from your goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. A 25-year-old saving for retirement can hold mostly equities and ride out the swings; a 60-year-old near retirement shifts toward bonds for stability. The old rule of thumb — "equity % ≈ 100 minus your age" — is crude but captures the direction: more time, more equity.

🇮🇳 India lens

A typical Indian household is heavily skewed to real estate and gold, often under-allocated to financial assets like equities and bonds. A balanced approach — equity mutual funds / index funds via SIP (M02), some debt funds or G-secs (M09), a slice of gold (SGBs), and adequate emergency cash — is the accessible, well-diversified path. Note that EPF/PPF and your home are already part of your allocation, even if you don't think of them that way.

Section 08Rebalancing & keeping discipline

Over time, your winners grow and your allocation drifts — a 60/40 portfolio after a stock rally might become 75/25, quietly far riskier than you intended. Rebalancing means periodically selling what's grown and buying what's lagged to restore your target mix.

★ Why rebalancing is secretly brilliant

Rebalancing mechanically forces you to sell high and buy low — trimming the asset that's run up, adding to the one that's down. It's a rule that does the emotionally hard thing automatically, defeating the very biases M16 is about. You don't have to feel brave; you just have to follow the rule. That's the quiet power of a systematic process over gut feeling.

Two more distinctions worth knowing: strategic allocation is your long-term target mix; tactical allocation is short-term tilts around it based on market views. For most people, a sound strategic allocation followed with discipline beats clever tactical moves — because, as M16 will show, our market timing is usually worse than we think.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. A portfolio's risk is less than the average of its parts' — that's why portfolios exist.
  2. Diversification is the only free lunch: lower risk without lower expected return.
  3. Correlation (+1 to −1) drives the benefit; the lower, the better the diversification.
  4. Return blends linearly; risk blends sub-linearly when correlation < 1.
  5. The efficient frontier is the set of max-return-per-risk portfolios (Modern Portfolio Theory).
  6. The Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of risk — the right comparison metric.
  7. Asset allocation drives most of your return variability — more than stock selection.
  8. Rebalancing restores your target mix and mechanically sells high / buys low.

Check yourself

Q1 · Why can adding a riskier asset (stocks) to a bond portfolio sometimes reduce total risk?
Because of low correlation. When stocks and bonds don't move together, their swings partly cancel, so a modest stock allocation can lower the portfolio's overall volatility while raising its return — the diversification "free lunch." You can see this bend in the frontier curve above.
Q2 · Two portfolios both return 10%. One has 8% volatility, the other 16%. Which is better, and by what measure?
The 8%-volatility one — it earns the same return for half the risk. The Sharpe ratio captures this: same numerator (excess return), smaller denominator (volatility) means a higher, better Sharpe. Always judge return relative to the risk taken.
Q3 · How does rebalancing enforce "buy low, sell high" automatically?
When an asset rises, it grows beyond its target weight, so rebalancing sells some of it (selling high). When an asset falls below target, rebalancing buys more (buying low). The rule does the counter-emotional thing for you, on schedule, without requiring willpower.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Portfolio
Your full set of investments treated as a single system.
Diversification
Combining imperfectly-correlated assets to reduce risk without cutting expected return.
Correlation
A measure (−1 to +1) of how two assets move relative to each other.
Volatility (σ)
Standard deviation of returns; the standard measure of risk.
Efficient frontier
The set of portfolios offering the highest return for each level of risk.
Modern Portfolio Theory
Markowitz's framework for building optimal portfolios via diversification.
Sharpe ratio
Excess return per unit of volatility; a reward-to-risk measure.
Asset allocation
The high-level split of a portfolio across asset classes.
Rebalancing
Restoring a portfolio to its target weights, selling winners and buying laggards.
Strategic vs tactical
Long-term target allocation vs short-term tilts around it.

Alternatives — the
other asset classes.

Stocks and bonds are the main course, but the financial world has a whole side table: real estate, private equity, commodities, currencies, and crypto. Each behaves differently, which is exactly the point. This module maps the alternative universe and shows where — and whether — it belongs in a portfolio.

Section 01What "alternatives" are & why they exist

"Alternative assets" is simply the catch-all for everything that isn't a publicly-traded stock or bond. They share a few family traits: they're often less liquid, harder to value, and less correlated with the stock market. Two real reasons investors bother with them:

  • Diversification — because they don't move in lockstep with stocks, adding them can smooth a portfolio's overall ride. (The deep math of this is M13.)
  • The illiquidity premium — assets you can't sell quickly should, in theory, pay you extra return to compensate for being locked in. You trade liquidity for yield.
⌘ Engineer's note

Correlation is the key variable. Two assets with low or negative correlation reduce combined volatility even if each is individually risky — the same reason redundant systems with independent failure modes are more reliable than duplicated ones with shared failure modes. Alternatives earn their place precisely when their returns are driven by different factors than equities. When they all crash together (2008, 2020), that diversification quietly evaporates — correlations spike toward 1 exactly when you need them low.

Section 02Real estate — direct & REITs

Property is the alternative asset most people already own (or aspire to). It generates returns two ways: rental income (a cash yield) and capital appreciation (the price rising). It's tangible, can be leveraged with a mortgage, and historically hedges inflation — but it's deeply illiquid, costly to transact, and concentrated (one flat is a huge, undiversified bet).

The market's clever fix is the REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust): a company that owns income-producing property and trades on the exchange like a stock. Buy a REIT unit and you own a slice of a diversified property portfolio — with daily liquidity and small ticket sizes. It turns an illiquid asset into a liquid one.

🇮🇳 India lens

Indians' love of property and gold is cultural and deep — real estate is often the single largest household asset. Listed REITs (Embassy, Mindspace, Brookfield) now let you invest in commercial property from a demat account, and InvITs do the same for infrastructure (roads, power lines, telecom towers). Both are SEBI-regulated and pay regular distributions — a genuinely useful, accessible way to add real-estate exposure without buying a building.

Section 03Private equity & venture capital

Most companies aren't listed on an exchange. Private equity (PE) and venture capital (VC) are how investors fund and own these private businesses.

  • Venture capital — funding young, high-risk, high-growth startups in exchange for equity. Most fail; the rare winner returns the whole fund many times over. A power-law game.
  • Private equity — buying mature private companies (often using debt — a "leveraged buyout"), improving them, and selling at a profit years later.

Both are highly illiquid — your money is locked up for 7–10 years — and accessible mainly to wealthy or institutional investors. The promise is returns above public markets (the illiquidity premium in action); the reality is wide dispersion between the best and worst funds.

🇮🇳 India lens · AIFs

In India, PE/VC and other sophisticated strategies are packaged as Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs), regulated by SEBI in three categories — Cat I (VC, startups, infra), Cat II (PE, debt funds), Cat III (hedge-fund-style). They carry high minimum investments (typically ₹1 crore), reflecting that these are designed for sophisticated, high-net-worth investors, not the general public.

Section 04Commodities & gold

Commodities are raw physical goods — oil, metals, agricultural products, gold. They're unique among assets in that they generate no cash flow: a barrel of oil pays no dividend or coupon. Their return comes purely from price changes, driven by real-world supply and demand. Most commodity exposure is gained through futures (M10) rather than holding the physical good.

Their portfolio value is as an inflation hedge and diversifier — commodity prices often rise when inflation does, just as financial assets struggle. Gold is the special case: a millennia-old store of value that investors flee to in crises (a "safe haven"), even though it too pays nothing.

🇮🇳 India lens · gold

Gold isn't just an asset in India — it's culture, weddings, savings, and security rolled into one, and the country is among the world's largest consumers. Beyond physical jewellery, you can hold it as Sovereign Gold Bonds (issued by RBI, paying interest plus gold's price, a uniquely good structure), gold ETFs, or digital gold — all of which avoid the making-charges and storage headaches of physical metal.

Section 05Currencies — the FX market

The foreign exchange (FX or forex) market is the largest financial market on earth by volume — trillions of dollars traded daily — yet most individuals never touch it directly. Currencies are traded in pairs (USD/INR), and their relative values shift with interest rates, trade flows, inflation, and capital movements (the exchange-rate logic from M05).

For most people, FX matters as a risk to manage rather than an asset to hold: it affects importers, exporters, anyone with foreign income, and any investor holding overseas assets. Currency speculation is a professional, heavily-leveraged game best admired from a distance. Knowing how it works protects you; trying to trade it usually doesn't.

⌘ Engineer's note

An exchange rate is a relative price, not an absolute one — USD/INR rising means the dollar strengthened and the rupee weakened, two views of one number. There's no "true value," only the ratio between two economies' monetary conditions. That relativity is why FX feels slippery: there's no anchor, just two moving objects measured against each other.

Section 06Crypto — the newest, wildest class

Cryptocurrencies (Bitcoin, Ethereum, and thousands of others) are digital assets recorded on decentralized blockchains rather than issued by any central bank. They're the newest entrant and by far the most volatile — capable of doubling or halving in months. Proponents see digital gold and a new financial infrastructure; skeptics see a speculative asset with no cash flows and no intrinsic anchor.

⚠ A clear-eyed view

Crypto sits at the far end of the risk spectrum: no cash flows, extreme volatility, evolving regulation, and a history of fraud and collapse alongside the genuine innovation. It may have a role as a tiny, speculative slice of a portfolio for those who understand it — but size it as money you can afford to lose entirely. Treat anyone promising guaranteed crypto returns as a fraud, because they are.

🇮🇳 India lens · crypto tax

India hasn't banned crypto but taxes it punishingly: a flat 30% tax on gains (no deductions, no offsetting losses against other income), plus a 1% TDS on transactions above a threshold. Crypto is legal to hold and trade through registered exchanges, but the tax structure deliberately discourages active trading. Factor this in before assuming headline crypto returns are achievable after-tax.

★ The one law of the map

No asset escapes the trade-off: return is the reward for bearing risk. Anything claiming high returns with low risk is either mispriced (rare and fleeting), hiding its real risk (often), or a fraud (frequently). Your job as an investor isn't to find the impossible free lunch — it's to choose which risks you're paid to bear, and combine them so they don't all fail at once. That's the bridge into Part IV.

Section 08Where alternatives fit in a portfolio

So should you own alternatives? The honest, knowledgeable answer:

  • Core first. A sound portfolio is built on liquid stocks and bonds. Alternatives are seasoning, not the meal.
  • Mind the liquidity. Never put money you might need into something you can't sell for years. The illiquidity premium is only a gift if you can afford to be locked in.
  • Watch the costs & access. Many alternatives carry high fees and high minimums, and the dispersion between good and bad managers is enormous.
  • Accessible options exist. For most Indians, REITs, InvITs, gold (SGBs/ETFs), and a small managed allocation are the sensible, liquid ways to get alternative exposure — without the lock-ups of PE or the casino of crypto.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Alternatives = everything beyond public stocks/bonds; often illiquid, less correlated.
  2. Two reasons to hold them: diversification and the illiquidity premium.
  3. Real estate gives income + appreciation; REITs/InvITs make it liquid and accessible.
  4. PE/VC fund private companies — high return potential, long lock-ups (India: AIFs).
  5. Commodities & gold generate no cash flow; they hedge inflation and act as safe havens.
  6. FX is the largest market; for most people it's a risk to manage, not an asset to trade.
  7. Crypto is the most volatile class — tiny, speculative sizing only; India taxes it at 30% + 1% TDS.
  8. All assets obey one law: return is the reward for risk — build a core first, season with alternatives.

Check yourself

Q1 · What is the "illiquidity premium" and why should it exist?
It's the extra expected return investors demand for holding assets they can't easily sell. It should exist because being locked in is a real cost — you can't react to needs or opportunities — so rational investors require compensation for accepting it. Private equity's long lock-ups are the classic example.
Q2 · Why is gold considered a "safe haven" despite paying no income?
Because in crises investors flee to it — it holds value when financial assets and currencies wobble, has millennia of trust behind it, and isn't tied to any single government's solvency. Its lack of cash flow is irrelevant to that role; it's valued as a store of value and a hedge, not an income asset.
Q3 · Diversification from alternatives is said to "evaporate when you need it most." Why?
Because in severe crises (2008, 2020) correlations spike toward 1 — investors sell everything liquid at once, so assets that normally move independently fall together. The diversification benefit is real in normal times but weakest precisely during the panics it was meant to protect against.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Alternative assets
Investments outside public stocks and bonds: real estate, PE/VC, commodities, FX, crypto.
Illiquidity premium
Extra expected return for holding assets that can't be sold quickly.
REIT
Real Estate Investment Trust — an exchange-traded vehicle owning income-producing property.
InvIT
Infrastructure Investment Trust — the REIT structure applied to infrastructure assets.
Private equity (PE)
Investing in mature private companies, often via leveraged buyouts.
Venture capital (VC)
Funding early-stage startups for equity; a power-law, high-failure game.
Commodity
A raw physical good (oil, metals, grain, gold); generates no cash flow.
Safe haven
An asset (e.g. gold) investors move into during market stress.
Foreign exchange (FX)
The market for trading currencies, quoted as pairs (e.g. USD/INR).
AIF
Alternative Investment Fund — SEBI's regulated wrapper (Cat I/II/III) for sophisticated strategies in India.

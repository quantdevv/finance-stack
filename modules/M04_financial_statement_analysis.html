Reading & analyzing financial statements.

Section 01Why ratios — comparison is everything

"Net profit was ₹500 crore." Good or bad? You can't tell. A ₹500 cr profit is spectacular for a small firm and alarming for a giant. Raw numbers are meaningless in isolation — they only acquire meaning through comparison. That's what financial ratios do: they put one number over another to create a standardized, comparable measure.

Ratios let you compare a company against three things: itself over time (is it improving?), its competitors (is it winning?), and its industry norms (is it typical?). We group them into four families, each answering a core question about the business.

⌘ Engineer's note

Think of ratios as normalized metrics — like measuring requests-per-second instead of raw request counts, so you can compare a small service to a huge one. A single ratio is a data point; the signal is in the trend and the peer comparison. Never read one ratio in isolation, exactly as you'd never judge a system on one metric at one timestamp.

Section 02Liquidity — can it pay its bills?

Liquidity ratios ask the most immediate survival question: does the company have enough short-term assets to cover its short-term obligations? A profitable company that can't pay next month's bills is in serious trouble (remember M03: profit ≠ cash).

A current ratio above 1 means current assets exceed current liabilities — generally healthy. The quick ratio (or "acid test") is stricter: it strips out inventory, because inventory can be hard to convert to cash fast. If a retailer's current ratio looks fine but its quick ratio is weak, it's leaning heavily on unsold stock — a yellow flag.

Section 03Solvency & leverage — is the debt safe?

Liquidity is about the next year; solvency is about the long haul — can the company survive its total debt load? Debt is a double-edged sword (we'll see why in M06): it amplifies returns but can sink a company if it can't service it.

Debt-to-equity shows how much the company is funded by borrowing versus owners' money. A D/E of 2 means twice as much debt as equity — aggressive. Interest coverage is arguably more important: it shows how many times over the company's operating profit (EBIT) can cover its interest bill. A coverage of 1.5× is precarious; 8× is comfortable. When coverage drops near 1, the company is one bad quarter from not paying its lenders.

ROE is the headline number for shareholders — for every rupee owners put in, how many paise of profit does the company generate? ROA measures profit against all capital (debt + equity). The gap between them is created by leverage — which the next section makes beautifully concrete.

Section 05Efficiency — how hard do assets work?

Efficiency (activity) ratios measure how productively the company uses its assets to generate sales. The flagship is asset turnover = Revenue / Total Assets — how many rupees of sales each rupee of assets produces. A supermarket runs high turnover on thin margins; a luxury brand runs low turnover on fat margins. Neither is "better" — they're different strategies, and efficiency ratios reveal which game a company is playing. Related measures include inventory turnover (how fast stock sells) and receivables days (how fast customers pay).

Section 06DuPont — taking ROE apart

Here's the most elegant tool in statement analysis. ROE is the number everyone cares about, but on its own it hides how a company earns its return. The DuPont decomposition splits ROE into three drivers — profitability, efficiency, and leverage — that multiply together:

ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplierprofitability × efficiency × leverage

(The equity multiplier = Assets / Equity — a leverage measure. More debt → higher multiplier → higher ROE.) This is powerful because two companies can have the same ROE for completely different reasons — one through fat margins, another through heavy borrowing. DuPont exposes the difference. ⚠ The leverage illusion

Notice you can pump ROE up to dazzling levels just by sliding leverage. A company can look like a star performer purely because it borrowed heavily — not because its business is good. This is why analysts never trust ROE alone: a high ROE built on debt is fragile and risky. DuPont is precisely how you catch it.

Section 07Common-size & trend analysis

Two more techniques round out the toolkit. Common-size statements express every line as a percentage — income statement items as a % of revenue, balance sheet items as a % of total assets. This strips out size, so you can compare a ₹100 cr firm with a ₹10,000 cr firm directly, and instantly see, say, that one spends 40% of revenue on COGS while the other spends 60%.

Trend (horizontal) analysis looks at the same line over several years to spot direction: are margins expanding or eroding? Is debt creeping up? A single year is a snapshot; the trend is the story. Together, common-size and trend analysis turn a static report into a moving picture.

Section 08Quality of earnings & red flags

Finally, the detective work. Numbers can be technically accurate yet paint a misleading picture. Quality of earnings analysis asks: are these profits real, sustainable, and backed by cash? The single best test ties back to M03 — compare net income to operating cash flow. Over time they should track each other. When profits rise but cash doesn't, be suspicious.

  • Profit up, operating cash flow down — the classic warning. Earnings may be propped up by accounting, not real business.
  • Receivables growing faster than revenue — the company may be booking sales it hasn't collected (or stuffing the channel).
  • One-off gains dressed as recurring — selling a building isn't the same as selling product; watch for profit that won't repeat.
  • Rising debt funding dividends — paying shareholders with borrowed money is unsustainable.
  • Frequent "adjustments" and restatements — if a company constantly redefines its own numbers, trust them less.
🇮🇳 India lens

Several high-profile Indian collapses — Satyam (2009, fabricated cash), and more recent NBFC/bank stress cases — were visible in advance to anyone watching the gap between reported profit and real cash, or rising receivables and inter-company loans. You can pull any listed company's results from the BSE/NSE websites or its annual report and run exactly these checks. SEBI mandates the disclosures; the skill is reading them critically.

★ The analyst's mindset

Statement analysis isn't about computing ratios — software does that. It's about asking "what story do these numbers tell, and is it true?" Ratios are the questions; cash flow and trends are the cross-examination. You're now equipped to look at any company and form a defensible first opinion — which is exactly what equity research (M12) builds on.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Raw numbers are meaningless; ratios create comparison across time, peers, and industry.
  2. Liquidity (current, quick) — can it pay short-term bills?
  3. Solvency (debt/equity, interest coverage) — is the debt load safe?
  4. Profitability (margins, ROE, ROA) — how well does it convert sales to profit?
  5. Efficiency (asset turnover) — how hard do its assets work?
  6. DuPont splits ROE into margin × turnover × leverage — exposing the real driver.
  7. High ROE built on leverage is fragile, not impressive.
  8. Quality of earnings: profit should be backed by cash — the gap is the red flag.

Check yourself

Q1 · Two firms both have 20% ROE. One has 15% margins and low debt; the other has 4% margins and huge debt. Which is healthier?
The first. Same ROE, but DuPont shows the first earns it through genuine profitability, while the second manufactures it through leverage. The leveraged firm's return is borrowed and fragile — a downturn could wipe it out. Identical headline number, opposite quality.
Q2 · A retailer has a current ratio of 2.0 but a quick ratio of 0.6. What does that tell you?
Most of its current assets are inventory. It looks liquid on paper, but if that stock doesn't sell quickly, it could struggle to pay near-term bills. The gap between the two ratios is the warning.
Q3 · Net income is up 30% but operating cash flow fell. Concerned?
Yes. This is the classic quality-of-earnings red flag. Real profit should bring in cash. A widening gap suggests earnings may be inflated by accounting choices, uncollected sales, or one-offs rather than genuine business performance.

Glossary

Liquidity ratios
Measure ability to meet short-term obligations (current, quick ratio).
Solvency / leverage ratios
Measure long-term debt burden (debt-to-equity, interest coverage).
Profitability ratios
Measure profit relative to sales or capital (margins, ROE, ROA).
Efficiency ratios
Measure how productively assets generate sales (asset turnover).
ROE / ROA
Return on equity (owners' money) / return on assets (all capital).
DuPont analysis
Splits ROE into margin × turnover × leverage to find its source.
Common-size statement
Each line shown as a % of revenue or assets, for size-free comparison.
Quality of earnings
How real and cash-backed reported profits are.
Interest coverage
EBIT ÷ interest expense — how comfortably debt costs are covered.

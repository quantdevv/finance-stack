These words get used interchangeably but they're different activities with different mindsets:

Neither is superior — they're different games. But the trader's game is harder for individuals: shorter horizons mean more noise, more decisions, more costs, and far more emotional pressure. Going in clear-eyed about which game you're playing is itself an edge.

Section 02The premise of technical analysis

Technical analysis (TA) rests on three assumptions: (1) price discounts everything — all known information is already in the price, so study the price itself; (2) prices move in trends that persist; and (3) history rhymes — patterns recur because human psychology recurs.

⌘ Engineer's note

TA is essentially signal processing on a price time-series — smoothing, trend detection, momentum, mean-reversion. The honest framing: indicators are transformations of past prices designed to surface patterns. The contested question is whether those patterns have predictive power or are just the human pattern-recognition system finding faces in clouds. The academic consensus (M16's efficient markets) is skeptical; many practitioners disagree. Hold both views and stay empirical.

Section 03The chart vocabulary

The basic grammar every trader reads:

  • Trend — the prevailing direction: uptrend (higher highs & lows), downtrend, or sideways/range. "The trend is your friend."
  • Support & resistance — price levels where buying (support, a floor) or selling (resistance, a ceiling) has repeatedly emerged. Breaking through them is significant.
  • Volume — how many shares traded; confirms conviction behind a move. A breakout on high volume is more trustworthy than one on thin volume.
  • Candlesticks — each bar shows open, high, low, close for a period; their shapes (doji, hammer, engulfing) are read for sentiment.

Section 04Moving averages & crossovers

A moving average (MA) smooths price by averaging the last N periods, filtering out noise to reveal the trend. The classic signal is the crossover: when a fast (short-period) MA crosses a slow (long-period) MA, it may signal a trend change. A fast-crosses-above-slow is a bullish "golden cross"; the reverse is a bearish "death cross." Build it and place the signals yourself:

Play with the periods and watch what happens: shorter MAs react faster but fire more false signals (whipsaws) in choppy markets; longer MAs are smoother but signal late, after much of the move has happened. There's no free setting — it's the same speed-vs-accuracy trade-off as any filter. This built-in lag is exactly why crossover systems struggle in sideways markets, and why no indicator is a crystal ball.

Section 05Indicators — RSI & MACD

Beyond moving averages, two indicators dominate screens:

  • RSI (Relative Strength Index) — a 0–100 momentum gauge. Above ~70 is considered "overbought" (possibly due for a pullback); below ~30 "oversold." It measures the speed of recent gains vs losses.
  • MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) — tracks the relationship between two moving averages to flag momentum shifts; crossovers of its signal line are read as buy/sell cues.
⚠ Indicators don't predict — they describe

Every indicator is a mathematical function of past prices. None contains information that isn't already in the price history. "Overbought" can stay overbought for months in a strong trend. Treat indicators as ways to organise what you're seeing and enforce discipline — not as predictions. Stacking ten indicators doesn't create certainty; it creates confirmation bias with extra steps.

Section 06Patterns & their pitfalls

TA has a zoo of named chart patterns — head-and-shoulders, triangles, flags, double tops — each said to hint at the next move. Some traders swear by them; skeptics note that the human brain is a relentless pattern-finder that sees signal in random noise, and that many "patterns" are only obvious in hindsight.

★ The honest middle ground

Patterns may have modest value not because they're magic, but because they can become self-fulfilling — if enough traders act on the same pattern, their collective buying/selling makes it real for a while. That's a genuine effect, but a fragile one. The reasonable stance: know the vocabulary so you can read what others are reacting to, while staying deeply skeptical that any pattern is a reliable edge on its own.

Section 07Strategy, edge & expectancy

Profitable trading isn't about being right often — it's about positive expectancy: across many trades, average win × win-rate must exceed average loss × loss-rate, after costs.

Expectancy = (Win% × Avg win) − (Loss% × Avg loss)the only number that ultimately matters

You can be right just 40% of the time and still profit handsomely if your winners are much bigger than your losers — which is why the trader's mantra is "cut losses short, let winners run." The mechanics of survival are identical to M14: a strict stop-loss on every trade and risking only a tiny fraction of capital per position, so a losing streak can't wipe you out.

⌘ Engineer's note

A trading strategy is a system with a measurable edge (positive expectancy) that must survive real-world costs — spreads (M07), brokerage, taxes, slippage — which silently tax every transaction. Many strategies look profitable in backtests and die on contact with these frictions. Beware overfitting: tuning a strategy to past data until it looks perfect, then watching it fail live. It's the same generalization-vs-memorization trap as any model.

Section 08Why most traders lose

Time for the uncomfortable, well-documented truth. Across markets and studies, the large majority of active retail traders lose money over time. The reasons are structural, not bad luck: trading costs accumulate, they compete against professionals and algorithms with better tools and information, and — above all — human psychology (M16) sabotages them: overtrading, revenge trades, holding losers, cutting winners, and the leverage of F&O amplifying every error.

🇮🇳 India lens · the numbers are stark

SEBI's own studies found roughly 70% of intraday equity traders and over 90% of individual F&O traders (M10) lose money. Costs alone — STT, brokerage, exchange fees, GST — are a meaningful drag that the casual trader underestimates. None of this means you can't learn TA or trade thoughtfully; it means the realistic, knowledgeable stance is: most "trading" is gambling in disguise, the edge belongs to disciplined professionals, and for most people investing (M12–M13) beats trading. Knowing the tools protects you from those selling dreams with them.

★ What to actually take from this module

You don't need to become a trader to benefit. The payoff is literacy: you can now read a chart, understand what "support broke" or "golden cross" means, see through the seduction of indicator-stacking, and recognise that consistent trading profit requires an edge, discipline, and ruthless cost-awareness most people lack. That clear sight is exactly the goal — to never be blind to what's happening on a screen, or to who's really making money.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Trading (price, short horizon) is a different game from investing (value, long horizon).
  2. Technical analysis assumes price discounts all info, trends persist, and patterns recur.
  3. Chart grammar: trend, support/resistance, volume, candlesticks.
  4. Moving-average crossovers signal trend changes but always lag — speed vs accuracy.
  5. Indicators (RSI, MACD) describe past prices; they don't predict the future.
  6. Profit comes from positive expectancy, not high win-rate: cut losses, let winners run.
  7. Beware overfitting and the silent drag of costs (spreads, STT, brokerage).
  8. Most retail traders lose (SEBI: ~70% intraday, 90%+ F&O) — literacy protects you.

Check yourself

Q1 · Can you be profitable winning only 40% of your trades?
Yes — if your average winner is much larger than your average loser, expectancy can be positive despite a sub-50% win rate. This is the math behind "cut losses short, let winners run." Win rate matters less than the expectancy across many trades after costs.
Q2 · Why does a shorter moving average produce more false signals?
Because it reacts faster to every wiggle, including random noise. In choppy, sideways markets it crosses back and forth repeatedly ("whipsaws"), firing buy/sell signals that quickly reverse. Longer MAs filter noise better but signal late — the unavoidable speed-vs-accuracy trade-off you can see in the chart above.
Q3 · What is "overfitting" a trading strategy, and why is it dangerous?
It's tuning a strategy's rules to fit past data so closely that it captures noise rather than a real edge. It looks flawless in backtests but fails on new, live data because the patterns it memorised don't generalise. It's the same memorization-vs-learning failure as in any predictive model.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Technical analysis
Forecasting price moves from chart patterns, trends, and volume rather than fundamentals.
Trend
The prevailing direction of price (up, down, or sideways).
Support / resistance
Price levels where buying or selling has repeatedly emerged (a floor / a ceiling).
Moving average
The average price over the last N periods, used to smooth noise and show trend.
Crossover
When a fast MA crosses a slow MA, read as a potential trend-change signal.
RSI
Relative Strength Index — a 0–100 momentum gauge (overbought >70, oversold <30).
MACD
An indicator tracking the relationship between two moving averages to flag momentum shifts.
Expectancy
Average profit per trade across many trades: (win% × avg win) − (loss% × avg loss).
Stop-loss
A pre-set exit that caps the loss on a trade.
Overfitting
Tuning a strategy to past data so it fails on new data.

Section 01Why regulation exists

Left entirely alone, financial markets fail in predictable ways. Regulation exists to fix specific market failures:

  • Information asymmetry — companies know more than investors, creating room for deception. Disclosure rules force the truth out.
  • Fraud & manipulation — without rules, the powerful would cheat the rest. Laws punish it.
  • Systemic risk — one failure can cascade through the whole system (M14, M17), so authorities police stability.
  • Investor protection — ordinary people can't analyse everything, so minimum standards and safeguards protect them.
★ Regulation as the trust layer

Markets run on trust — you'll hand money to strangers (M07) only if you believe the system is fair. Regulation manufactures that trust at scale: it's the reason you'll buy a share from an unknown seller, deposit savings in a bank, or trust a company's accounts. Without credible rules, markets shrink to people who already know each other. The rules are what make finance big enough to fund an economy.

Section 02The regulators — who guards what

India's financial system is policed by specialist regulators, each owning a domain:

RegulatorDomain
SEBISecurities markets — stocks, mutual funds, brokers, exchanges
RBIBanking, monetary policy, payments, NBFCs (M17)
IRDAIInsurance
PFRDAPensions (NPS)
MCA / ICAICompany law & accounting/audit standards (M03)

For an investor, SEBI is the one you'll encounter most: it registers and supervises everyone in the securities chain, mandates disclosures, investigates fraud and insider trading, and runs investor-education and grievance systems. Its existence is why Indian markets are far safer than the wild, scam-ridden bazaars of decades past (the 1992 Harshad Mehta scam was a key spur for SEBI's empowerment).

Section 03Market protections — disclosure & insider trading

Two pillars protect the ordinary investor:

  • Disclosure — listed companies must regularly publish audited financials (M03), material events, and risks, so all investors decide on the same information. Sunlight is the cheapest regulation.
  • Insider-trading prohibition — trading on material, non-public information (e.g. a director buying shares before an unannounced great result) is illegal. It would let insiders fleece outsiders and destroy trust in a fair market.
▸ Why insider trading is policed so hard

If insiders could legally trade on secret information, every outsider would be playing a rigged game and would rationally leave — collapsing the market's liquidity and price discovery (M07). The ban isn't about fairness as sentiment; it's about keeping the market functional. Market abuse more broadly — pump-and-dump schemes, spreading false news, manipulating prices — is policed for the same structural reason.

Section 04Corporate governance

Corporate governance is the system of rules and practices ensuring a company is run in the interests of all its owners, not just insiders. You met its failures already — Satyam (M03), IL&FS (M09) — and its analysis (M12's promoter question). Its key machinery:

  • The board of directors — elected by shareholders to oversee management, ideally with independent directors who aren't beholden to the promoters.
  • Auditors — independent accountants verifying the financials (only as good as their independence — Satyam's auditors failed).
  • Minority-shareholder protections — rules against insiders extracting value via related-party transactions, ensuring small holders aren't exploited.

Section 05Ethics & fiduciary duty

Beyond law lies ethics — the professional standards that bind people who manage others' money. The central concept is fiduciary duty: a legal and moral obligation to act in your client's best interest, ahead of your own. A financial advisor with a fiduciary duty must recommend what's best for you, not what pays them the biggest commission.

⚠ The conflict to always watch for

Not everyone selling you financial products is a fiduciary. Many "advisors" are really salespeople earning commissions — incentivised to sell high-fee products (recall M16's sell-side conflicts). In India, distinguish a SEBI Registered Investment Advisor (RIA), who owes you fiduciary duty for a fee, from a commission-earning distributor. Always ask: "How are you paid?" The answer reveals whose interest your advisor truly serves.

⌘ Engineer's note

The CFA Institute's Code of Ethics (M21) makes this rigorous — placing client interests first, full disclosure of conflicts, integrity of markets. Think of ethics as the protocol layer on top of the legal layer: law sets the hard floor (what's punishable), ethics sets the higher standard professionals commit to. In a field built on trust and handling other people's money, reputation is the asset — and it's destroyed in one breach.

Section 06How investments are taxed in India

Now the intensely practical part. When you make money from investments, the government taxes it — and the rules differ sharply by asset and holding period. The two flavours of capital gains:

  • Short-term capital gains (STCG) — gains on assets held below a threshold period; taxed at higher rates.
  • Long-term capital gains (LTCG) — gains on assets held longer; taxed at lower, preferential rates to reward patient investing.

The threshold and rate depend on the asset class. There's also STT (a small tax on every market transaction, M10/M15), tax on dividends and interest (at your slab), and the punitive crypto regime (M11). The key idea: holding longer usually means paying less tax — the tax code itself nudges you toward investing over trading.

⚠ Tax rules change — verify before you act

Tax law is among the most frequently changed parts of finance; the rules below reflect a recent framework (the regime tightened materially in the July 2024 Budget) but rates, thresholds, and exemptions shift with each year's Budget. Treat the calculator as illustrative, not authoritative — always confirm current rules with the latest Budget or a qualified CA (M20) before making decisions. This isn't tax advice.

Section 08Tax-efficient investing

Smart investors don't just chase returns — they keep more of them by being tax-aware (legally; evasion is illegal, planning is not):

  • Hold for the long term to access lower LTCG rates and let compounding (M02) run untaxed until you sell.
  • Use tax-advantaged wrappers — in India, ELSS funds, PPF, EPF, and NPS offer deductions (Section 80C) and/or tax-free growth.
  • Harvest the LTCG exemption — the annual long-term equity exemption can be used each year by booking gains up to the limit.
  • Mind asset location — hold tax-inefficient assets in tax-advantaged accounts where possible.

Across M17–M19 you've seen the scaffolding that holds finance up: banks creating and channelling money, quants modelling its risks, and regulators + the tax code shaping everyone's behaviour. None of it makes headlines, but all of it determines the environment in which your investing actually happens. Knowing the scaffolding is what separates someone who's merely heard of markets from someone who genuinely understands how the system works — which was the whole point.

Check yourself

Q1 · Why is insider trading banned — is it just about fairness?
It's deeper than fairness: if insiders could trade on secret information, outsiders would be in a rigged game and would rationally exit, destroying the market's liquidity and price discovery (M07). The ban keeps the market functional, not merely "nice." Trust is the product regulation protects.
Q2 · Your "advisor" earns commissions on the products they sell you. What's the concern, and what should you ask?
They may not be a fiduciary — they're incentivised to sell high-commission products rather than what's best for you. Ask "How are you paid?" A fee-only SEBI Registered Investment Advisor (RIA) owes you a fiduciary duty; a commission-earning distributor does not. The payment model reveals the conflict.
Q3 · Why does India tax long-term capital gains at a lower rate than short-term?
To reward patient, long-term investing over short-term trading and speculation — it's a deliberate policy nudge. The lower LTCG rate (plus an annual exemption on equity) means holding past the long-term threshold cuts your tax, reinforcing the same long-horizon discipline that M13–M16 advocate. Crypto is the punitive exception: flat 30% regardless.

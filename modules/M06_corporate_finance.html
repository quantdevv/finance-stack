Section 01The three questions of corporate finance

Strip a CFO's entire job down and it's three decisions, repeated forever:

  • The investment decision — which projects, assets, or ventures should we put money into? (Capital budgeting)
  • The financing decision — should we fund them with debt, equity, or retained profits? (Capital structure)
  • The distribution decision — what do we do with profits: reinvest them or return them to shareholders? (Dividend policy)

Every one of these has the same north star: maximize the value of the firm — which, from M02, means maximize the present value of its future cash flows. Corporate finance is just the rigorous application of "does this create value?" to each decision. Let's take them in order.

Section 02Capital budgeting: NPV & IRR

A company has limited money and many possible projects — a new factory, a product line, an acquisition. Capital budgeting is how it decides which are worth doing. And the master tool is one you already met: Net Present Value (NPV).

The logic is exactly M02: a project costs cash now and (hopefully) returns cash over future years. Discount every future cash flow back to today, sum them, subtract the upfront cost. If what comes back — in today's money — exceeds what you put in, the project creates value.

NPV = Σ [ CFt ÷ (1 + r)t ] − Initial Investmentaccept if NPV > 0

Alongside NPV sits the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) — the discount rate at which NPV equals exactly zero. Think of it as the project's built-in annual return. ⚠ NPV beats IRR when they disagree

IRR is intuitive (a single % return) but has traps: it can give misleading or multiple answers with unusual cash-flow patterns, and it doesn't account for project size. When NPV and IRR conflict, professionals trust NPV — it directly measures rupees of value created. IRR is the friendly summary; NPV is the truth.

A cruder third method, payback period (how many years to recoup the cost), is sometimes used as a quick filter — but it ignores the time value of money and everything beyond the payback point, so it's a rough screen, not a decision rule.

Section 03The cost of capital & WACC

Everything above hinges on the discount rate r — but where does a company get it? It's the company's cost of capital: the return its investors require for providing money. Since a firm funds itself with a mix of debt and equity, its overall rate is a blend — the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).

WACC = (E/V)·Re + (D/V)·Rd·(1−T)blended cost of equity and after-tax debt

In words: take the cost of equity (what shareholders demand) and the cost of debt (the interest rate on borrowing), weight each by its share of total funding, and blend. Two things to notice, both important:

  • Debt is cheaper than equity. Lenders take less risk than owners (they get paid first), so they demand a lower return. Equity is the most expensive money a company can raise.
  • Debt gets a tax shield. Interest is tax-deductible — that's the (1−T) term. So debt is even cheaper after tax. This is a core reason companies borrow at all.
⌘ Engineer's note

WACC is the project's hurdle rate — the minimum return that clears the bar. Any project returning more than WACC creates value; anything less destroys it. It's a threshold check: if (project_return > WACC) ship();. The whole NPV machine is just this comparison, done precisely across time.

Section 04Capital structure: debt vs equity

If debt is cheaper, why don't companies fund everything with debt? This is the capital structure question — the mix of debt and equity — and it's one of the deepest in finance. The answer is a trade-off:

  • More debt lowers WACC (cheaper, tax-advantaged money) and amplifies returns to shareholders — up to a point.
  • More debt raises risk. Interest must be paid no matter what; miss it and you face bankruptcy. Beyond some level, the danger of financial distress outweighs the cheapness.

There's a famous theoretical result — the Modigliani–Miller theorem — that in a perfect world (no taxes, no bankruptcy costs) capital structure wouldn't matter at all. Its real value is in showing why it matters in the real world: precisely because of taxes (which favour debt) and distress costs (which punish too much of it). The optimal structure balances the tax benefit of debt against the rising risk of bankruptcy. There's no universal answer — a stable utility can carry lots of debt; a volatile startup should carry almost none.

Section 05The double edge of leverage

Leverage — using borrowed money — is the amplifier of corporate finance. You saw a glimpse in M04's DuPont tool. Here's the intuition made concrete:

▸ Worked example — leverage cuts both ways

You buy a ₹1 crore asset. Case A (no debt): all your money. If it rises 10%, you make ₹10 lakh on ₹1 cr — a 10% return.

Case B (50% debt): you put in ₹50 lakh, borrow ₹50 lakh. If it rises 10% (₹10 lakh gain), your return on your ₹50 lakh is ~20% (minus interest). Leverage doubled your gain.

But if the asset falls 10%, Case A loses 10% while Case B loses ~20% of your capital — and you still owe the full loan. Leverage magnifies gains and losses symmetrically. It's the single most important risk concept in all of finance.

★ Why this echoes everywhere

Leverage is the thread connecting corporate finance, banking (M17), trading (M15), and every financial crisis you'll study. Companies, banks, traders, and homeowners all use it to amplify returns — and excessive, hidden leverage is what turns a downturn into a catastrophe. When you understand leverage deeply, you understand the engine behind most financial blow-ups.

Section 06Dividend policy: pay or reinvest?

When a company earns a profit, it faces the distribution decision: return cash to shareholders, or keep it to reinvest? Two ways to return it:

  • Dividends — direct cash payments to shareholders, usually regular. Signal stability and maturity.
  • Share buybacks — the company buys back its own shares, raising the value of the rest. A more flexible, often tax-efficient alternative.

The deciding logic: a company should reinvest profits only if it can earn more than its cost of capital on them (i.e. find positive-NPV projects). If it can — a young, growing firm — shareholders are better off having it reinvest. If it can't — a mature firm with few good projects — it should return the cash and let shareholders deploy it elsewhere. A growth company that pays no dividend (reinvesting everything) and a mature company that pays a fat one can both be acting correctly.

🇮🇳 India lens

You'll see this split clearly on Indian exchanges: fast-growing firms (many newer tech and consumer names) reinvest aggressively and pay little; mature cash-cows (large IT services firms, FMCG, PSUs) pay steady dividends and do buybacks. Neither is "better" — it reflects where each sits in its growth cycle. India taxes dividends in the investor's hands, which influences the dividend-vs-buyback choice.

Section 07Working capital — the daily plumbing

The big decisions get headlines, but companies die from a humbler problem: running out of cash to operate day-to-day. Working capital management is the unglamorous art of keeping the lights on — managing the short-term assets and liabilities from M03.

The key idea is the cash conversion cycle: a company pays for inventory and labour before it collects from customers, creating a cash gap it must fund. Good working-capital management shrinks that gap — collect from customers faster, manage inventory tighter, pay suppliers on sensible terms. Get it wrong and even a profitable, growing company can choke (the "growing broke" trap, straight out of M03's profit-isn't-cash lesson).

Section 08Mergers & acquisitions (a first look)

Sometimes the best "investment" a company can make is buying another company. M&A is corporate finance at its most dramatic, and it's evaluated the same way as any project: does the deal create value? The logic is synergy — the idea that the combined firm is worth more than the two apart (through cost savings, new markets, or market power). The hard truth, well-documented, is that many acquisitions destroy value — buyers overpay, synergies don't materialize, integration fails. The discipline you've learned (NPV, cost of capital, honest cash-flow forecasting) is exactly what separates a smart acquisition from an ego-driven one. We'll see the investment-banking side of M&A in M17.

★ The unifying thread

Every section here reduces to one test: does this decision earn more than the cost of capital? Invest if the project's return beats WACC. Borrow if the tax-advantaged cheapness outweighs the added risk. Reinvest profits only if you can beat your cost of capital; otherwise pay them out. Acquire only if synergies justify the price. Corporate finance is value creation, measured one decision at a time — and it all rests on M02.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Corporate finance answers three questions: invest, finance, distribute.
  2. Capital budgeting uses NPV (accept if > 0) and IRR (accept if > cost of capital).
  3. When NPV and IRR conflict, trust NPV — it measures real value created.
  4. The discount rate is the WACC — the blended cost of debt and equity.
  5. Debt is cheaper (lower risk + tax shield) but adds bankruptcy risk.
  6. Capital structure balances debt's cheapness against distress risk.
  7. Leverage amplifies gains and losses symmetrically — the core risk concept.
  8. Reinvest only if you can beat your cost of capital; otherwise return cash.

Check yourself

Q1 · A project has an IRR of 9% and the company's WACC is 12%. Do it?
No. The project returns 9% but the company's investors require 12% to fund it. It earns less than the cost of capital, so its NPV is negative — it destroys value. Reject it.
Q2 · Why is equity more expensive than debt for a company?
Because equity holders take more risk — they're paid last (after lenders, employees, suppliers) and have no guaranteed return. To compensate, they demand a higher return. Plus, debt interest is tax-deductible while dividends aren't, making debt cheaper still on an after-tax basis.
Q3 · A profitable young company pays zero dividends. Is that bad for shareholders?
Not necessarily — likely the opposite. If it can reinvest profits at a return above its cost of capital (positive-NPV growth), shareholders are better off letting it compound internally than taking cash. Paying dividends would only make sense once it runs out of high-return projects.

Glossary

Capital budgeting
Deciding which long-term projects to invest in.
NPV / IRR
Net present value (rupees of value created) / the rate where NPV = 0.
Cost of capital / WACC
The return investors require; the blended cost of debt and equity.
Hurdle rate
The minimum return a project must clear (usually the WACC).
Capital structure
The mix of debt and equity a firm uses to fund itself.
Leverage
Use of borrowed money; amplifies both gains and losses.
Tax shield
The tax saving from debt, since interest is tax-deductible.
Dividend policy
How a firm splits profit between payouts and reinvestment.
Working capital
Short-term assets minus liabilities; funds day-to-day operations.
Synergy
The idea that a merged firm is worth more than its parts.

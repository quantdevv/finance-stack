Accounting — the language of business.

Section 01Why accounting exists

In M02 you learned that a company's value is the present value of its future cash flows. Fine — but where do those numbers come from? How do you know what a business earns, what it owns, whether it's drowning in debt? You can't peer inside ten thousand companies yourself. So we need a standardized report card. That's accounting: the system for recording, summarizing, and communicating a business's financial activity in a format anyone can read.

Think of it as the API for a company's finances — a common schema that lets outsiders (investors, lenders, regulators, you) query the health of a business without being inside it. Every public company publishes this report, audited for honesty, on a fixed schedule. Master the format once and you can read Reliance, Apple, or your neighbour's startup with the same eye.

★ Key idea

There are exactly three financial statements, and they answer three different questions: the Income Statement ("did we make a profit?"), the Balance Sheet ("what do we own and owe right now?"), and the Cash Flow Statement ("where did the actual cash move?"). Together they're a complete X-ray. Most of this module is just learning to read each one — and crucially, how they lock together.

Section 02The one equation that always balances

All of accounting rests on a single, unbreakable identity. Burn it in:

Assets = Liabilities + Equitythe accounting equation

Read it plainly: everything a company owns (assets) was paid for either with borrowed money (liabilities) or with the owners' own money (equity). There's no third source. So the two sides must always be equal — every rupee of asset is claimed by either a lender or an owner.

  • Assets — what the company controls that has value: cash, inventory, machines, buildings, money owed to it (receivables).
  • Liabilities — what it owes to others: loans, unpaid bills, money it owes suppliers (payables).
  • Equity — the owners' stake: what's left for shareholders after all debts are settled. Also called net worth or book value.

Rearrange it and you get the most honest definition of ownership there is: Equity = Assets − Liabilities. What you truly own is what's left after what you owe. (That's exactly how your personal net worth works too.)

Section 03Double-entry & the balance demo

Because the equation must always hold, every transaction has to touch at least two accounts in a way that keeps both sides equal. This is double-entry bookkeeping, invented by a Venetian monk in 1494 and unchanged in spirit since. Buy a machine with cash? One asset (cash) goes down, another (equipment) goes up — assets unchanged, still balanced. Take a loan? Cash (asset) up, loan (liability) up — both sides rise together.

⌘ Engineer's note

Double-entry is a built-in integrity constraint — like a database invariant or a checksum that must hold after every write. If your books don't balance, you know there's a bug (an error or fraud) before you trust the data. The terms "debit" and "credit" are just the left/right sides of each entry; don't let the jargon intimidate you — they're bookkeeping mechanics, not deep concepts. The invariant Assets = Liabilities + Equity is the real idea.

Section 04The Income Statement — did we make money?

The Income Statement (also called Profit & Loss, or P&L) covers a period of time — a quarter, a year — and answers: did the business make a profit? It's a waterfall that starts with sales at the top and subtracts costs step by step until you reach the bottom line.

  • Revenue (top line) — total sales the company earned.
  • − Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) — direct cost of making what it sold → Gross Profit.
  • − Operating expenses (salaries, rent, marketing) → Operating Income (EBIT) — profit from the core business.
  • − Interest (cost of debt) and Taxes → Net Income (the bottom line).

Each level tells you something different. Gross profit shows whether the product itself is profitable. Operating income (EBIT) shows whether the business model works, ignoring how it's financed. Net income is what's actually left for owners. You'll hear "EBITDA" too — operating income before depreciation/amortization, a rough proxy for cash earnings.

⚠ Profit is an opinion

Here's the trap that catches everyone: net income is not cash. A company can report a fat profit and still go bankrupt for lack of cash, because the income statement records sales when earned, not when collected (Section 07). "Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact." That's why we need the cash flow statement.

Section 05The Balance Sheet — what do we own & owe?

If the income statement is a video of a period, the Balance Sheet is a photograph at a single instant — "as at 31 March 2026." It lists everything the company owns and owes at that moment, and (of course) it balances: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

Assets and liabilities are each split by time horizon:

  • Current assets — turn to cash within a year (cash, receivables, inventory). Non-current assets — long-term (property, plant, equipment, patents).
  • Current liabilities — due within a year (payables, short-term debt). Non-current liabilities — long-term debt.
  • Equity — paid-in capital plus accumulated retained earnings (profits kept in the business over the years).

The balance sheet is where you spot financial health and danger: too much debt vs equity, not enough current assets to cover current liabilities, bloated inventory. M04 is entirely about squeezing these judgments out of it.

Section 06The Cash Flow Statement — where did cash go?

The third statement exists to fix the "profit isn't cash" problem. The Cash Flow Statement tracks the actual movement of cash over the period, sorted into three buckets:

  • Operating activities (CFO) — cash from the core business. The most important number: a healthy company generates real cash here.
  • Investing activities (CFI) — cash spent on or earned from long-term assets (buying machines, acquisitions). Usually negative for a growing firm.
  • Financing activities (CFF) — cash from/to investors and lenders (issuing shares, borrowing, repaying debt, paying dividends).

Add the three and you get the net change in cash for the period — which must reconcile exactly to the change in the cash line on the balance sheet. This statement is the lie-detector: if profits are rising but operating cash flow isn't, something's off (Section 04 of M04 covers these red flags).

Section 07Accrual vs cash: the big idea

This is the concept that separates people who "get" accounting from those who don't. There are two ways to record activity:

  • Cash basis — record revenue/expense only when cash actually moves. Simple, but misleading: it ignores money owed to and by you.
  • Accrual basis — record revenue when it's earned and expenses when they're incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands. This is what GAAP and IFRS require, because it matches effort to reward and shows the true economic picture.
▸ Worked example — accrual in action

You deliver software in March but the client pays in May. Under accrual, you record the revenue in March (when earned) — even though no cash arrived. The unpaid amount sits on the balance sheet as accounts receivable (an asset: money owed to you). When cash arrives in May, receivables fall and cash rises — no new revenue, just a conversion. This is precisely why profit and cash diverge, and why you need all three statements.

Two accrual mechanics worth naming: Depreciation spreads the cost of a long-lived asset (a machine) across the years it's used, instead of expensing it all at once. Amortization does the same for intangibles (patents, software). Both are real expenses on the income statement but involve no cash going out in that period — a key reason cash flow differs from profit.

Section 08How the three statements connect

This is the payoff. The three statements aren't separate — they're one interlocking system, and seeing the links is what makes you literate:

  • Net income from the income statement flows into retained earnings in the equity section of the balance sheet.
  • Ending cash from the cash flow statement becomes the cash line on the balance sheet.
  • Depreciation appears as an expense on the income statement and reduces asset values on the balance sheet, while being added back on the cash flow statement (since it's non-cash).
🇮🇳 India lens

Indian companies report under Ind AS (Indian Accounting Standards), which are converged with global IFRS; US companies use US GAAP. The differences matter for professionals (and show up heavily in the CA and CFA syllabi — M20, M21), but the three-statement structure and the balancing equation are universal. Every listed Indian company files these statements with audited accounts — you can pull Reliance's or TCS's annual report and read it with exactly what you've learned here.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Accounting is the standardized report card of a business — its financial API.
  2. Everything rests on Assets = Liabilities + Equity, which always balances.
  3. Double-entry is a built-in integrity check: every transaction touches two sides.
  4. The Income Statement shows profit over a period (revenue − costs = net income).
  5. The Balance Sheet is a snapshot of what's owned and owed at one instant.
  6. The Cash Flow Statement tracks real cash — the lie-detector for profit.
  7. Accrual records earned/incurred, not cash timing — why profit ≠ cash.
  8. The three statements interlock: profit → equity; ending cash → balance sheet.

Check yourself

Q1 · A company has ₹10 cr assets and ₹6 cr liabilities. What's its equity?
₹4 cr. Equity = Assets − Liabilities = 10 − 6. That's the owners' true stake — what would remain if every debt were paid off.
Q2 · Can a profitable company go bankrupt?
Yes. Profit (accrual) records sales when earned, not collected. If a firm books big profits but customers haven't paid (huge receivables) while its own bills come due, it can run out of cash despite being "profitable." Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact.
Q3 · You buy a ₹50,000 machine with cash. What happens to total assets?
Nothing — total assets are unchanged. Cash (an asset) falls ₹50,000 and equipment (an asset) rises ₹50,000. One asset converts into another; the equation stays balanced. This is double-entry in action.
Q4 · Why is depreciation added back on the cash flow statement?
Because it's a non-cash expense. It reduced net income on the income statement, but no cash actually left the company that period (the cash went out when the asset was bought). So to get from profit to real cash, you add it back.

Glossary

Accounting equation
Assets = Liabilities + Equity; the identity that always holds.
Double-entry bookkeeping
Every transaction recorded in two places to keep the books balanced.
Income Statement (P&L)
Reports revenue, expenses, and profit over a period.
Balance Sheet
Snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time.
Cash Flow Statement
Tracks actual cash via operating, investing, and financing activities.
Accrual accounting
Records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, not when cash moves.
Receivables / Payables
Money owed to the company (an asset) / money the company owes (a liability).
Depreciation / Amortization
Spreading the cost of a tangible / intangible asset over its useful life.
Retained earnings
Cumulative profits kept in the business; part of equity.
GAAP / IFRS / Ind AS
The rulebooks for accounting (US / international / India).

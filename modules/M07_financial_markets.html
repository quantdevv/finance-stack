Section 01What a financial market actually is

Back in M01 you saw the financial system's job: move money from people who have surplus (savers) to people who can use it (borrowers and businesses). A financial market is simply any place — physical or electronic — where that transfer happens through buying and selling financial instruments: shares, bonds, currencies, derivatives, and so on.

A market does three quiet but vital jobs: it lets capital find its best use (allocation), it lets you turn an asset back into cash quickly (liquidity), and through millions of buy/sell decisions it sets a price — the market's collective opinion of what something is worth right now.

★ The one-line model

A market is a price-discovery machine. Every trade is two people disagreeing about the future but agreeing on a price. The constant tug-of-war between buyers and sellers produces the single number we call "the price" — and it updates the instant new information arrives.

Section 02Money markets vs capital markets

Markets are first split by how long the money is tied up.

  • Money markets — very short-term debt, maturing in under a year. Treasury bills, commercial paper, certificates of deposit, overnight inter-bank lending. Low risk, low return, highly liquid. This is where governments, banks, and big firms park or borrow cash for days or weeks.
  • Capital markets — long-term funding, over a year. This splits into the equity market (shares — ownership) and the debt/bond market (loans — lending). This is where companies raise money to build, and where most investing happens.
🇮🇳 India lens

India's money market is overseen by the RBI; the capital market by SEBI. When the RBI changes the repo rate (M01), it's acting directly on the money market — the short end — and that ripples out into every other rate. Instruments you'll hear about: T-bills (91/182/364-day), commercial paper, and the call money market where banks lend each other money overnight.

Section 03Primary vs secondary markets

This distinction trips up a lot of people, so make it stick: it's about who gets the money.

  • Primary market — securities are created and sold for the first time. The company (or government) gets the cash. An IPO (Initial Public Offering) is the classic example: a firm sells new shares to the public and the proceeds fund the business.
  • Secondary market — existing securities trade between investors. The company gets nothing; money moves from one investor to another. When you buy a share on the NSE, you're buying it from another investor, not from the company.
⌘ Engineer's note

Think of the primary market as a mint (new units issued, issuer funded) and the secondary market as a resale marketplace (units change hands, original issuer untouched). Most of what people call "the stock market" is the secondary market — but the secondary market's liquidity is exactly what makes anyone willing to buy in the primary market. They depend on each other.

Section 04Exchanges vs over-the-counter

Where do trades physically happen? Two models:

  • Exchange-traded — a centralized, regulated venue with standardized contracts, transparent prices, and an order book everyone trades against. Stocks and listed derivatives trade this way. Think NSE, BSE.
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) — a decentralized web of direct, negotiated deals between two parties (often via dealers). Customizable but less transparent and with more counterparty risk. Most bonds, currencies (forex), and many derivatives trade OTC.

The trade-off is standardization & safety (exchange) vs flexibility & customization (OTC). A retail equity investor lives almost entirely in the exchange world; a corporate treasurer hedging a specific currency exposure may need the OTC world.

Section 05The participants — who's in the room

A market is its people. The main cast:

  • Retail investors — individuals like you, trading your own money.
  • Institutional investors — mutual funds, pension funds, insurers, banks, foreign portfolio investors (FPIs). They move huge sums and dominate volume.
  • Brokers — licensed intermediaries who route your order to the exchange (Zerodha, Groww, ICICI Direct). You can't trade on the exchange directly; you go through a broker.
  • Market makers / dealers — firms that continuously quote both a buy and a sell price, providing liquidity and earning the spread between them.
  • Custodians — hold securities safely on behalf of large investors.
  • Clearing corporations & depositories — the back-office plumbing (Section 08) that guarantees and records every trade.
  • Regulators — SEBI (markets), RBI (money/banking), setting and enforcing the rules.

Section 06Order types, bid-ask & liquidity

When you trade, you send an order. The two you must know:

  • Market order — "fill me right now at whatever the best available price is." Fast, but you don't control the exact price.
  • Limit order — "fill me only at this price or better." You control price, but it may not execute.

At any moment the market shows a bid (highest price buyers will pay) and an ask/offer (lowest price sellers will accept). The gap between them is the bid-ask spread — a direct, real cost of trading and a thermometer of liquidity.

▸ Why liquidity matters

A blue-chip like Reliance has a spread of a few paise — you can buy and sell instantly at almost the same price. A thinly-traded small-cap might have a spread of 1–2%, meaning you lose money the instant you buy. Liquidity is invisible until you need it — and in a panic, illiquid assets are the ones you can't sell at any sensible price.

Section 07Indices — Nifty, Sensex & what they measure

An index is a single number that tracks a basket of stocks, giving you the market's overall mood at a glance. India's two headline indices:

  • Nifty 50 — 50 large companies on the NSE, weighted by free-float market cap.
  • Sensex — 30 large companies on the BSE, India's oldest index (since 1986).

"The market is up 1%" almost always means an index moved 1%. Indices also became something you can invest in directly — index funds and ETFs simply hold the basket, a cornerstone idea you'll meet again in M13 (portfolios) and M21 (the CFA worldview).

⌘ Engineer's note

An index is a weighted aggregate — a single scalar summarizing a high-dimensional system, exactly like a benchmark score. "Free-float market-cap weighted" means each company's influence is proportional to the value of shares actually available to trade. Change the weights and you change what the number even means — which is why different indices can disagree on the same day.

Section 08The plumbing — clearing & settlement

Here's what almost nobody understands, and you're about to. When you tap "buy," the trade isn't truly done at the moment of matching. Behind the scenes a careful relay race runs to make sure the buyer actually gets shares and the seller actually gets money — without either having to trust the other. Walk through it:

Stage 1 — You place the order
You tap "Buy 10 shares" in your broker app. Nothing has been bought yet — you've just sent an instruction.
🇮🇳 India lens · the demat era

Your shares don't exist as paper — they live electronically in a demat account at a depository: NSDL or CDSL. The clearing corporation (NSE's NCL) steps in as the guaranteed counterparty to every trade, so you never rely on the stranger on the other side. And since 2023, India runs on a T+1 cycle — settlement one business day after the trade — among the fastest in the world. (A move toward optional T+0 is underway.)

★ Why the plumbing is genius

The clearing corporation's role as central counterparty means the system has no single point of trust. Buyer and seller never have to know or trust each other — they each only face the clearing corp, which guarantees the trade. It's the financial equivalent of an escrow service running billions of times a day. This is what makes a market feel instant and safe even though it's strangers trading with strangers.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. A market is a price-discovery machine doing three jobs: allocation, liquidity, pricing.
  2. Money markets = short-term (under 1yr); capital markets = long-term (equity + debt).
  3. Primary market funds the issuer (IPO); secondary market is investors trading with each other.
  4. Exchanges (standardized, transparent) vs OTC (negotiated, flexible).
  5. You reach the market through a broker; institutions dominate the volume.
  6. The bid-ask spread is a real trading cost and a measure of liquidity.
  7. An index (Nifty, Sensex) is a weighted basket summarizing the market in one number.
  8. Clearing & settlement (T+1, NSDL/CDSL, clearing corp) is the trust-free plumbing behind every trade.

Check yourself

Q1 · You buy 10 shares of Infosys on the NSE. Does Infosys receive your money?
No. That's a secondary-market trade — your money goes to another investor who sold. Infosys only received cash when it originally issued those shares in the primary market (its IPO / further issues).
Q2 · Why does a wide bid-ask spread cost you money?
Because you buy at the higher ask and would have to sell at the lower bid. The gap is an immediate loss you must overcome before you even break even — and it's wider for illiquid (thinly-traded) securities.
Q3 · What does the clearing corporation do, and why does it matter?
It acts as the central counterparty — the guaranteed buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer. This removes counterparty risk: you never have to trust the stranger on the other side, only the clearing corp, which is heavily regulated and capitalized.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Money market
Market for short-term debt (under one year): T-bills, commercial paper, call money.
Capital market
Market for long-term funding: equities (shares) and bonds (debt).
Primary market
Where new securities are first issued; the issuer receives the funds (e.g. an IPO).
Secondary market
Where existing securities trade between investors; the issuer is not involved.
OTC
Over-the-counter — decentralized, negotiated trading between parties, off-exchange.
Bid / Ask
Highest price buyers will pay / lowest price sellers will accept; the gap is the spread.
Liquidity
How easily an asset can be sold quickly without moving its price.
Index
A weighted basket of securities expressed as one number (Nifty 50, Sensex).
Depository (NSDL/CDSL)
Electronic registries holding shares in demat form.
Clearing & settlement
The process of confirming, guaranteeing, and finalising trades (India: T+1).

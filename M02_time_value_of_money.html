Section 01Why a rupee today beats a rupee tomorrow

Offer someone ₹1,000 now or ₹1,000 in a year, and everyone picks now. That instinct is correct, and finance turns it into precise math. There are three reasons money has a time value, and you already met all of them in M01:

  • Opportunity — ₹1,000 today can be invested and grow. Wait a year and you've lost a year of earning. The rupee now is a working rupee.
  • Inflation — prices rise, so ₹1,000 next year buys less than ₹1,000 today. The future rupee is a weaker rupee.
  • Risk & uncertainty — a promise of future money might not be kept. A rupee in hand is certain; a rupee promised is not.

So money has a time value: the same number means different real worth at different moments. To compare amounts across time, we need a way to move money forward and backward through time — and the engine that does it is compounding (forward) and discounting (backward). That's this whole module.

★ Why this is THE foundation

Here is the secret that makes finance learnable: the value of any asset is the present value of all the cash it will generate in the future. A stock, a bond, a rental flat, an entire company — all of them are just future cash flows, pulled back to today. Once you can do that arithmetic, valuation stops being magic. Every module from M06 onward leans on what you learn here.

Section 02Future value & the miracle of compounding

Future value (FV) answers: if I invest money today at some rate, what will it be worth later? The mechanism is compounding — earning returns on your returns. After year one you earn interest on the principal; after year two you earn interest on the principal plus last year's interest, and so on. It snowballs.

FV = PV × (1 + r)n future value

PV = amount today  ·  r = rate per period  ·  n = number of periods

That little exponent n is where the magic lives. Compare it to simple interest (FV = PV × (1 + r×n)), where you only ever earn on the original principal — a straight line. Compounding is exponential, and over long horizons the gap becomes staggering.

▸ Worked example — the staggering gap

Invest ₹10,000 at 8% for 30 years.

Simple interest: 10,000 × (1 + 0.08×30) = 10,000 × 3.4 = ₹34,000.

Compound interest: 10,000 × (1.08)30 = 10,000 × 10.06 = ₹1,00,627.

Same rate, same money, same time. Compounding produces nearly three times as much. The difference is entirely "interest on interest." This is why Einstein supposedly called compounding the eighth wonder of the world — and why starting early matters more than almost anything else in personal finance.

⌘ Engineer's note

Compounding is just exponential growth — the same (1+r)n you'd recognize from population models or Moore's law. Mentally: balance = balance * (1 + r) run in a loop, n times. Simple interest is linear; compound is geometric. The "miracle" is nothing mystical — it's the difference between O(n) and exponential, applied to your savings account.

A shortcut worth memorizing: the Rule of 72

Want to know how fast money doubles? Divide 72 by the rate. At 8%, money doubles in about 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years. At 12%, about 6 years. It's an approximation, but it's accurate enough to do in your head and it builds real intuition for what a rate means over time.

Section 03Present value & discounting

Now run the machine backwards. If future value asks "what will today's money become?", present value (PV) asks the far more useful question: "what is future money worth today?" This single skill — pulling future cash back to the present — is the workhorse of all valuation.

It's just the FV formula rearranged. Instead of multiplying by (1+r)n, you divide by it. That division is called discounting, and r in this direction is the discount rate.

PV = FV ÷ (1 + r)n present value · "discounting"
▸ Worked example — what's a future promise worth now?

Someone will pay you ₹10,00,000 in 20 years. If you could otherwise earn 8%, what's that promise worth to you today?

PV = 10,00,000 ÷ (1.08)20 = 10,00,000 ÷ 4.66 = ₹2,14,548.

So you should be indifferent between ₹2.14 lakh now and ₹10 lakh in 20 years — because ₹2.14 lakh invested at 8% becomes ₹10 lakh in 20 years. They're the same value, just at different times. That's the whole trick: time-travel for money.

The crucial intuition from that picture: the further in the future a cash flow is, the less it's worth today. A rupee in year 30 is worth far less than a rupee next year. This is why long-dated promises (and far-off profits) get heavily discounted, and it explains a lot of market behaviour you'll see later.

Section 04The discount rate — the most important number

Notice that everything hinges on r. Change the discount rate and the value of every future cash flow changes. So what is the discount rate, really? It's the return you require to part with your money — your opportunity cost of capital. And it's built from the same three ingredients as any interest rate (M01):

r ≈ risk-free rate + inflation comp. + risk premium what goes into a discount rate

The riskier or more uncertain the future cash, the higher the rate you demand, and the more you discount it. This is the hinge between this module and all of investing: two analysts can look at the identical company and disagree on its value purely because they used different discount rates.

⚠ The relationship to burn in

Higher discount rate → lower present value. They move in opposite directions, always. This is why, when central banks raise interest rates (M01's master lever), stock and bond prices tend to fall — a higher rate discounts all those future cash flows more heavily, so they're worth less today. You now understand one of the deepest links in all of finance. Keep it close.

Section 05Compounding frequency: APR vs APY

So far we've compounded once a year. But interest often compounds more often — monthly, daily, even continuously. The more frequently it compounds, the more you earn, because your interest starts earning interest sooner. This creates a trap you must learn to see through.

  • APR (nominal rate) — the stated annual rate, ignoring within-year compounding. The number on the advertisement.
  • APY / EAR (effective rate) — the true annual rate after accounting for compounding frequency. The number that actually hits your account.
EAR = (1 + r/m)m − 1 effective annual rate · m = compounding periods/year
▸ Worked example — the credit-card trap

A card quotes 12% APR, but compounds monthly. The real annual rate is:

EAR = (1 + 0.12/12)12 − 1 = (1.01)12 − 1 = 12.68%.

You're actually paying 12.68%, not 12%. Lenders advertise the APR; you should always compute the EAR. The more often something compounds against you (debt) the worse the trap — and the more often it compounds for you (investments) the better.

🇮🇳 India lens

This is why bank FDs advertise both a "rate" and an "annualised yield" — the yield is the EAR after quarterly compounding. And it's the legal reason loan ads must disclose the APR. When you compare two FDs or two loans, compare effective rates, never the headline number, especially if their compounding frequencies differ.

Section 06Annuities — streams of equal payments

Real life rarely involves one lump sum. It involves streams — a salary every month, an EMI every month, a SIP every month, a pension every year. An annuity is a series of equal payments at regular intervals. Rather than discounting each payment one by one, there's a clean formula:

PV = C × [ 1 − (1 + r)−n ] ÷ r present value of an annuity · C = payment per period
FV = C × [ (1 + r)n − 1 ] ÷ r future value of an annuity (e.g. a SIP)

You don't need to memorize these to understand finance — any calculator or spreadsheet does them (PV(), FV(), PMT() in Excel). What you need is to recognize the shape: whenever you see "equal payments over time," it's an annuity, and it has a present value and a future value.

▸ Worked example — the SIP that builds a fortune

You invest ₹5,000/month for 20 years (240 months) at a 12% annual return (1% per month). Future value of the annuity:

FV = 5,000 × [(1.01)240 − 1] ÷ 0.01 ≈ 5,000 × 989 = ₹49.5 lakh.

You put in only ₹12 lakh (5,000 × 240). The other ~₹37.5 lakh is pure compounding. This single calculation is the entire case for disciplined, early, regular investing — and it's why the SIP is the most quietly powerful tool a regular Indian investor has.

⌘ Engineer's note

An annuity formula is just the closed-form sum of a geometric series — the same collapse from a for-loop of discounted terms into one expression. EMIs work identically in reverse: the bank solves the annuity PV equation for C given the loan amount, rate, and tenure. Your home-loan EMI is literally PMT(rate, n, principal).

Section 07Perpetuities — money forever

Push an annuity to its limit: payments that continue forever. That's a perpetuity. It sounds like it should be worth infinity, but it isn't — because those far-future payments are discounted so heavily they add almost nothing. The math collapses to something beautifully simple:

PV = C ÷ r present value of a perpetuity

₹1,000 every year forever, discounted at 5%, is worth just 1,000 ÷ 0.05 = ₹20,000 today. That's it. And if the payments grow at a steady rate g, you get the growing perpetuity: PV = C ÷ (r − g).

★ Why you'll see this again

That growing-perpetuity formula is the seed of the Gordon Growth Model used to value stocks (M08), where C is next year's dividend, r is the required return, and g is the dividend growth rate. A huge chunk of equity valuation is just this one line dressed up. You're already holding the key to it.

Section 08Net Present Value — the master decision rule

Here's where it all pays off. Suppose an investment costs money now and returns cash over several future years. How do you decide if it's worth it? You discount every future cash flow back to today, add them up, and subtract what you paid. That's Net Present Value (NPV):

NPV = Σ [ CFt ÷ (1 + r)t ] − initial cost net present value

The rule is simple and absolute: if NPV > 0, the investment creates value (it returns more, in today's money, than it costs). If NPV < 0, it destroys value. This single rule is how companies decide whether to build factories, how investors decide whether a deal is good, and how you can decide whether any financial choice makes sense.

We'll use NPV properly in M06 (Corporate Finance), but recognize what just happened: with present value, the discount rate, and a sum, you now have a universal yardstick for comparing any set of cash flows across time. That is the entire reason this module sits at the base of the curriculum.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Money has a time value — driven by opportunity, inflation, and risk.
  2. Compounding (FV = PV(1+r)n) is exponential and dwarfs simple interest over time.
  3. Discounting (PV = FV/(1+r)n) pulls future money to today — the core valuation skill.
  4. An asset's value = the present value of its future cash flows.
  5. The discount rate is opportunity cost; higher rate → lower present value, always.
  6. Always compare effective (EAR/APY) rates, not headline APR.
  7. Annuities (equal streams: EMIs, SIPs) and perpetuities (C/r) have clean formulas.
  8. NPV > 0 means an investment creates value — the master decision rule.

Check yourself

Q1 · Why is ₹10 lakh in 20 years worth only ~₹2 lakh today (at 8%)?
Because of discounting. That ₹2 lakh, invested at 8%, would itself grow into ₹10 lakh over 20 years — so they're equivalent. The further away a cash flow, the more heavily it's divided by (1+r)n, so the less it's worth now.
Q2 · A card says 24% APR, compounded monthly. Roughly what are you really paying?
EAR = (1 + 0.24/12)12 − 1 = (1.02)12 − 1 ≈ 26.8%. Monthly compounding pushes the true cost well above the headline 24%. Always compute the effective rate.
Q3 · The central bank raises interest rates. Why do stock and bond prices tend to fall?
Higher rates mean a higher discount rate. Since an asset's value is the present value of its future cash flows, discounting those cash flows more heavily makes them worth less today — so prices fall. Higher r → lower PV.
Q4 · In a ₹5,000/month SIP that grows to ₹49.5 lakh over 20 years, where did the money come from?
You contributed only ₹12 lakh (5,000 × 240 months). The remaining ~₹37.5 lakh is compounding — returns earning returns. This is the entire argument for investing early and regularly.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Time value of money
The principle that money available now is worth more than the same amount later.
Future value (FV)
What an amount today will grow to, via compounding: FV = PV(1+r)n.
Present value (PV)
What a future amount is worth today, via discounting: PV = FV/(1+r)n.
Compounding
Earning returns on previously earned returns; produces exponential growth.
Discounting
Reversing compounding — converting future cash to its present value.
Discount rate
The required return used to discount; reflects opportunity cost, inflation, and risk.
APR vs EAR/APY
APR = stated nominal rate; EAR/APY = true rate after within-year compounding.
Annuity
A series of equal payments at regular intervals (EMIs, SIPs, pensions).
Perpetuity
An annuity that pays forever; PV = C/r (or C/(r−g) if growing).
Net Present Value (NPV)
Sum of discounted future cash flows minus cost; positive means value created.

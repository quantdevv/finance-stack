Section 01The two halves: micro & macro

Economics is the study of how people and societies allocate scarce resources. It splits cleanly into two scales, and finance draws on both:

  • Microeconomics — the small picture. Individual buyers, sellers, and firms; how prices form; why a company prices a product the way it does. This shapes how you analyze a single business.
  • Macroeconomics — the big picture. Whole economies; growth, inflation, unemployment, interest rates, government policy. This shapes the environment every investment lives in.

You don't need to become an economist. You need the handful of mechanisms that actually drive financial markets — and they're surprisingly few. Let's build them from the ground up, starting with the single most important idea in all of micro.

Section 02Supply, demand & price

Almost every price on earth — for goods, labour, money, stocks — emerges from two opposing forces meeting. Demand slopes downward: as price rises, buyers want less. Supply slopes upward: as price rises, sellers offer more. Where the two curves cross is the equilibrium — the price and quantity at which the market clears, with no shortage or surplus.

The real insight isn't the static crossing point — it's what happens when a curve shifts. A new technology, a tax, a trend, a supply shock: each moves a whole curve, and the equilibrium slides to a new price and quantity. Drag the sliders below to feel it:

Notice the patterns: a rise in demand pushes both price and quantity up; a rise in supply pushes quantity up but price down. This single mental model explains oil prices, housing, wages, and — crucially — the price of money itself (interest rates are just the price where the supply of savings meets the demand for borrowing).

⌘ Engineer's note

Equilibrium is a feedback system settling to a stable state. Too-high price → surplus → downward pressure; too-low price → shortage → upward pressure. The market is a distributed controller with price as the error signal, converging on the clearing point. No central coordinator needed — exactly the kind of self-balancing system you'd appreciate.

Section 03Elasticity — how sensitive is demand?

Curves aren't all equally steep, and the steepness matters enormously. Price elasticity of demand measures how much quantity responds to a price change.

  • Elastic demand — quantity is very sensitive to price (luxuries, things with substitutes). Raise the price a little and sales collapse. Think branded clothing.
  • Inelastic demand — quantity barely moves with price (necessities, addictive goods, things with no substitute). Petrol, salt, life-saving medicine, cigarettes.

This is why governments tax inelastic goods (you'll keep buying petrol regardless), why pharma can charge high prices, and why a company with "pricing power" (inelastic demand for its product) is so valuable to investors — it can raise prices without losing customers. When we value companies later, pricing power is one of the most prized qualities.

Section 04Market structures — competition to monopoly

How much pricing power a firm has depends on its market structure — how much competition it faces:

  • Perfect competition — many tiny sellers of identical products (commodities, agriculture). No one has pricing power; everyone takes the market price. Thin margins.
  • Monopolistic competition — many sellers, but differentiated products (restaurants, clothing brands). Some pricing power via branding.
  • Oligopoly — a few large players dominate (telecom, airlines, cement). They watch each other closely; pricing is strategic.
  • Monopoly — one seller, no substitutes. Maximum pricing power (often regulated, like utilities).
★ The investor's translation

Warren Buffett calls it the "economic moat." A business with less competition and more pricing power earns higher, more durable profits. When you analyze a company (M12), one of the first questions is: what's its market structure, and how protected is it from competition? Economics gives you the vocabulary to answer that.

Section 05GDP & the business cycle

Now zoom out to macro. GDP (Gross Domestic Product) is the total value of all goods and services an economy produces in a period — the single broadest measure of economic size and health. It's often expressed as a simple sum of who spends:

GDP = C + I + G + NXconsumption + investment + govt spending + net exports

When GDP grows, the economy is expanding — more jobs, more profits, rising markets. When it shrinks for two consecutive quarters, that's the technical definition of a recession. Economies don't grow in a straight line; they move in a business cycle:

Section 06Inflation & unemployment

You met inflation in M01 — the sustained rise in prices that erodes money's value. Macro adds its uneasy partner: unemployment, the share of people who want work but can't find it. These two are the central bank's twin obsessions, because they often pull in opposite directions.

The rough historical relationship (the "Phillips curve") suggests that when unemployment is low, the economy runs hot and inflation tends to rise; when unemployment is high, inflation tends to cool. This creates the fundamental policy dilemma: fight inflation and you risk unemployment; fight unemployment and you risk inflation. Every central bank decision is a negotiation between these two. (The relationship is messier in reality — "stagflation," high inflation and high unemployment together, broke the simple version in the 1970s — but the tension is real.)

Section 07Monetary vs fiscal policy — the two levers

Governments and central banks have two distinct toolsets to steer the economy. Confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in reading financial news, so get this clear:

  • Monetary policy — run by the central bank (RBI, Fed). Tools: interest rates and the money supply. To stimulate: cut rates, making borrowing cheap. To cool: raise rates. This is the lever from M01.
  • Fiscal policy — run by the government. Tools: spending and taxation. To stimulate: spend more or cut taxes (putting money in people's hands). To cool: spend less or raise taxes.
MONETARY = central bank · rates & money  |  FISCAL = govt · spend & taxthe two levers of economic policy

Both aim to smooth the business cycle — boosting a sluggish economy or restraining an overheating one. They can work together or at cross-purposes. When you hear "the RBI cut the repo rate" (monetary) versus "the Budget raised infrastructure spending" (fiscal), you now know exactly which lever is being pulled and by whom.

🇮🇳 India lens

In India, monetary policy is set by the RBI's Monetary Policy Committee (the repo rate is the headline tool, aimed at the 4% inflation target). Fiscal policy is set by the government, most visibly in the annual Union Budget each February — tax changes and spending plans. Markets watch both intensely: an RBI rate decision and Budget day are two of the most market-moving events on the Indian calendar.

Section 08Exchange rates & the global picture

No economy is an island. An exchange rate is simply the price of one currency in terms of another (₹/$), and like any price, it's set by supply and demand for the currencies. When more people want to buy Indian goods, assets, or rupees, the rupee strengthens; when capital flows out, it weakens.

Why it matters to finance: a weaker rupee makes Indian exports cheaper (good for IT and pharma exporters) but imports — including oil — more expensive (feeding inflation). Currency moves ripple through company earnings, inflation, and central bank decisions. The broader accounting of a country's transactions with the world is the balance of payments, but the headline you need is simpler: exchange rates are prices, driven by the same supply-and-demand forces as everything else, just at a national scale.

★ Tying it together

Every macro concept here connects back to markets. Supply & demand sets prices. GDP and the cycle set the backdrop. Inflation and unemployment drive central bank decisions. Those decisions move interest rates — which, via M02, reprice every asset on earth. You now have the macro scaffolding to understand why a single inflation report can move trillions in markets worldwide.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Economics splits into micro (firms & prices) and macro (whole economies).
  2. Supply & demand set prices; shifts in either move the equilibrium.
  3. Elasticity measures price sensitivity — inelastic demand = pricing power.
  4. Market structure (competition → monopoly) determines a firm's pricing power and moat.
  5. GDP measures economic size; economies move in a business cycle.
  6. Inflation and unemployment usually trade off — the central bank's core dilemma.
  7. Monetary policy = central bank (rates); fiscal = government (spend & tax).
  8. Exchange rates are prices set by supply and demand, with economy-wide effects.

Check yourself

Q1 · A drought wrecks the wheat harvest. What happens to wheat's price and quantity?
Supply shifts left (less available). The equilibrium moves to a higher price and lower quantity. Because food demand is inelastic, the price spike is sharp — people still need to eat, so they pay more rather than buy much less.
Q2 · The RBI cuts rates and the government raises infrastructure spending. Which is which?
The rate cut is monetary policy (central bank, cost of money). The spending increase is fiscal policy (government, spend & tax). Both are stimulative — they're pulling in the same direction to boost growth.
Q3 · Why do investors prize a company with "pricing power"?
Pricing power means inelastic demand for its product — usually from a strong brand, monopoly position, or lack of substitutes. It can raise prices without losing customers, protecting profits and margins (an "economic moat"). That makes its earnings higher and more durable.

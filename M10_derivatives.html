Derivatives — value
derived from elsewhere.

A derivative has no value of its own; it borrows value from something else — a stock, an index, a commodity, a currency. They're the most powerful and most dangerous instruments in finance: brilliant for managing risk, ruinous when misused. Let's demystify them, payoff diagram by payoff diagram.

Section 01What "derived value" means

A derivative is a contract whose value depends on — is derived from — the price of some other asset, called the underlying. The derivative itself is just an agreement; its worth rises and falls with the thing it references. A contract tied to the Nifty, to gold, to the rupee-dollar rate, to a single stock — all derivatives.

⌘ Engineer's note

Think of a derivative as a function whose output tracks an input variable — the underlying is the argument, the derivative's payoff is the return value. The "shape" of that function (linear for futures, kinked for options) is the whole game. Once you see derivatives as payoff functions of the underlying price, the jargon collapses into a handful of graph shapes. That's why this module is built around diagrams.

Section 02Forwards & futures — locking a price

The simplest derivative: an agreement to buy or sell something at a fixed price on a future date, regardless of the market price then. This lets you lock in certainty today.

  • Forward — a private, customized OTC contract between two parties. Flexible, but with counterparty risk.
  • Future — a standardized, exchange-traded version. The clearing corporation (M07) guarantees it, removing counterparty risk, and it's marked-to-market daily.
▸ The classic example — a farmer

A wheat farmer fears prices will fall by harvest. A bread maker fears they'll rise. They agree today on a fixed price for delivery in three months. Now both have certainty — the farmer locks in revenue, the baker locks in cost. Neither is gambling; they've each removed a risk from their business. This is the honest, original purpose of derivatives.

A future's payoff is linear: every rupee the underlying moves, you gain or lose a rupee (per unit). Symmetric upside and downside. Simple — and that simplicity is exactly why options, which break the symmetry, are so interesting.

Section 03Options — the right, not the obligation

An option is the pivotal idea in derivatives. Unlike a future (which obligates both sides), an option gives the buyer a right without an obligation — they can walk away. For that privilege, the buyer pays an upfront premium. Two flavours:

  • Call option — the right to buy the underlying at a fixed price (the strike). You buy a call when you expect the price to rise.
  • Put option — the right to sell the underlying at the strike. You buy a put when you expect the price to fall (or to insure something you own).

The asymmetry is everything. An option buyer's loss is capped at the premium paid, while the potential gain can be large. The option seller (writer) has the mirror image: they collect the premium but take on large, sometimes unlimited, risk. This lopsided shape is what makes options both insurance and lottery ticket depending on how you use them.

★ Read the kinks

Every option payoff is a bent line — flat on one side, sloped on the other, with the bend at the strike. The premium just shifts the whole line up (if you sold it) or down (if you bought it). Four shapes cover the basics: long call (▁╱), long put (╲▁), short call (▔╲), short put (╱▔). Combine them and you can build any risk profile you want — that's what "options strategies" really are.

Section 05The three reasons anyone uses them

Every derivative trade, however exotic, is one of three things:

  • Hedging — reducing an existing risk. An exporter hedges the rupee; an investor buys puts as portfolio insurance. This is risk removal, the farmer's use.
  • Speculation — taking on risk for profit, betting on a price direction with leverage. This is risk seeking.
  • Arbitrage — exploiting a price discrepancy between markets for near-riskless profit. This is what keeps related prices consistent and is the theoretical engine behind all derivative pricing.
⌘ Engineer's note

The same instrument is insurance or gambling depending entirely on whether you already hold the underlying risk. A put on a stock you own is a hedge; a put on a stock you don't own is a speculation. The contract is identical — the context determines everything. This is the distinction regulators and risk managers care about most.

Section 06Leverage — the double-edged sword

Derivatives let you control a large position with a small amount of money. A ₹10,000 options position can give you exposure to ₹5,00,000 of stock. That's leverage — and it multiplies both gains and losses. A 2% move in the underlying might be a 100% gain or a 100% loss on your premium.

small capital × large exposure = amplified outcomesthe leverage equation, both directions
⚠ The thing that ruins people

Leverage is why derivatives blow up accounts, banks, and occasionally the whole system (2008 was, in part, a derivatives story). The same feature that makes them efficient hedging tools makes them devastating when used to gamble. The instrument is neutral; the leverage is not forgiving. Respect it, and never trade what you don't understand.

Section 07Swaps — exchanging cash flows

A swap is an agreement to exchange one stream of cash flows for another. The most common, an interest-rate swap, trades a fixed interest rate for a floating one — letting a company that has a floating-rate loan but wants predictability effectively convert it to fixed (and vice versa). Currency swaps exchange payments in different currencies. Swaps are huge, OTC, and mostly the domain of institutions, but the concept is simple: I'll take your kind of payment if you take mine.

Section 08The Indian F&O reality

India runs one of the world's most active derivatives markets, traded on the NSE as F&O (Futures & Options) — on indices like the Nifty and Bank Nifty, and on individual stocks. Contracts trade in fixed lot sizes (you can't buy a single unit), and the leverage is real.

🇮🇳 India lens · read this carefully

SEBI studies have found that the vast majority of individual F&O traders lose money — a 2024 study put it around 90%+ in net losses, with most of the profits going to a few large institutional players. Retail options trading exploded after cheap apps arrived, and SEBI has been steadily tightening rules (higher contract sizes, fewer weekly expiries) to cool the frenzy. Add costs like STT (Securities Transaction Tax) and brokerage, and the odds get worse. Treat F&O as a professional risk-management tool, not a get-rich scheme — which is exactly how a knowledgeable person thinks about it.

★ What to actually take away

You don't need to trade derivatives to benefit from understanding them. Their real value to you is conceptual: they reveal how risk can be unbundled, priced, and transferred — the deepest idea in modern finance. A bond has credit risk and rate risk bundled together; derivatives let you separate and trade each piece. Once you see finance as the business of slicing and repricing risk, the entire field becomes legible.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. A derivative's value is derived from an underlying asset; it's a payoff function of that price.
  2. Forwards/futures lock a price (linear, symmetric payoff); futures are the exchange-traded, guaranteed version.
  3. Options give a right without obligation for a premium — asymmetric payoffs.
  4. Calls bet up, puts bet down; buyers risk only the premium, sellers take large risk.
  5. Payoff diagrams are bent lines kinked at the strike — read the shape, understand the position.
  6. Three uses: hedging (remove risk), speculation (seek it), arbitrage (exploit mispricing).
  7. Leverage amplifies gains and losses alike — the source of both efficiency and ruin.
  8. India's F&O market is huge but most retail traders lose; treat derivatives as tools, not bets.

Check yourself

Q1 · You own Infosys shares and buy a put option on them. Hedge or speculation?
Hedge. The put gives you the right to sell at the strike, capping your downside on shares you actually own — that's insurance. The very same put bought without owning the shares would be a speculation on the price falling.
Q2 · Why is a long option buyer's maximum loss limited, but an option seller's risk large?
The buyer paid a premium for a right they can abandon — worst case, they lose the premium. The seller received the premium but took on the obligation to deliver if exercised, so their loss grows as the market moves against them (potentially unlimited for a naked short call).
Q3 · A ₹10,000 options bet controls ₹5,00,000 of stock. The stock moves 2%. Roughly what could happen to your ₹10,000?
A 2% move on ₹5,00,000 is ₹10,000 — equal to your entire stake. So that small underlying move could roughly double or wipe out your position. That's leverage: outcomes amplified far beyond the underlying's percentage move.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Derivative
A contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset's price.
Underlying
The asset a derivative references (stock, index, commodity, currency).
Forward / Future
An agreement to trade at a fixed price on a future date; futures are standardized and exchange-traded.
Option
A contract granting the right, not obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike price.
Strike price
The fixed price at which an option can be exercised.
Premium
The upfront price the option buyer pays the seller.
Payoff diagram
A graph of profit/loss versus the underlying's price at expiry.
Hedging / Speculation / Arbitrage
Removing risk / seeking risk for profit / exploiting price discrepancies.
Leverage
Controlling a large exposure with small capital, amplifying gains and losses.
Swap
An agreement to exchange one stream of cash flows for another (e.g. fixed for floating).

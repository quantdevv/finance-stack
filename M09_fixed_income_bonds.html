M09 · Lending, Not Owning

Fixed income
& the world of bonds.

The bond market is bigger than the stock market — and quieter, which is why most people ignore it. That's a mistake. Bonds are where interest rates, central banks, and risk all meet, and once you grasp the one inverse relationship at their core, a huge amount of finance clicks into place.

Section 01What a bond actually is

If a share is owning a piece of a company, a bond is lending money to one. A bond is simply an IOU: you hand over cash today, and the issuer contractually promises to pay you regular interest and return your principal on a set date. You're the lender; the company or government is the borrower.

This flips the risk-return deal from M08. As a bondholder you're higher in the queue than shareholders (you get paid first) and your returns are fixed and known — hence "fixed income." The trade-off: no ownership, no vote, and no share of the upside if the company soars. You get your promised payments and nothing more.

★ Fixed claim vs residual claim

Remember M08's mental model: a bond is a fixed claim (a known amount, paid first) and a share is a residual claim (whatever's left, paid last). Lower risk, capped reward for the bondholder; higher risk, uncapped reward for the shareholder. Every difference between the two instruments flows from this one line.

Section 02Anatomy: par, coupon, maturity

Every bond is defined by a few fixed terms:

  • Face / par value — the principal the issuer repays at the end (commonly ₹1,000 or ₹100).
  • Coupon rate — the annual interest, as a % of face value. A 7% coupon on ₹1,000 pays ₹70 a year (often split into two ₹35 half-yearly payments).
  • Maturity — the date the principal is repaid and the bond ends. Could be months or 30+ years.
  • Issuer — who's borrowing (government, company, bank) — which determines the credit risk.

Here's the subtlety that confuses everyone: the coupon is fixed forever, but the bond's market price moves. Once issued, a bond trades in the secondary market (M07), and its price changes daily even though its coupon never does. Understanding why is the heart of this module.

Section 03Pricing a bond = PV of its cash flows

A bond is the cleanest possible application of M02. Its cash flows are completely known: a stream of coupons plus the face value at maturity. So its fair price is just the present value of all those cash flows, discounted at the market's current required return:

Price = Σ [ Coupon ÷ (1+y)t ] + Face ÷ (1+y)n bond price · y = market yield, n = periods

That's it — a bond is an annuity (the coupons) plus a lump sum (the face value), both discounted. You've been able to do this since M02. The only new variable is y, the yield — the market's currently demanded return, which moves with interest rates and the issuer's risk.

⌘ Engineer's note

A bond is a deterministic cash-flow schedule run through the present-value function. There's no guessing about amounts (unlike a stock's dividends) — only about the discount rate. That's what makes bond math precise and bond pricing a closed-form calculation. The entire fixed-income world is the PV formula plus careful bookkeeping about y.

Section 04Yield to maturity & the inverse relationship

The yield to maturity (YTM) is the single discount rate that makes the present value of a bond's cash flows equal its current market price — effectively, the total annualized return you earn if you buy now and hold to maturity. It's the bond market's true "price," quoted instead of the rupee price because it's comparable across bonds.

Now the most important fact in all of fixed income. Look at the pricing formula: y is in the denominator. So when yields rise, prices fall, and when yields fall, prices rise. They move in opposite directions, always. This is the famous inverse price-yield relationship. Feel it for yourself:

▸ Why it must be inverse — the intuition

Suppose you hold a bond paying a 7% coupon, and new bonds start being issued at 9% (because rates rose). Nobody wants your stingy 7% bond at full price anymore — so its price must fall until its effective yield matches the new 9%. The fixed coupon can't change, so the price does all the adjusting. Rates up → old bonds worth less. This is exactly why bond funds lose money when the RBI hikes rates.

Section 05Duration — measuring interest-rate risk

Not all bonds react equally to yield changes. A 30-year bond's price swings far more than a 2-year bond's for the same yield move, because more of its value sits in distant, heavily-discounted cash flows. Duration measures this sensitivity: roughly, a duration of 8 means a 1% rise in yields drops the price about 8%.

% price change ≈ −Duration × (change in yield)the working rule of interest-rate risk

Longer maturity and lower coupons both mean higher duration — more risk from rate moves. This single number is how bond investors and risk managers (M14) quantify their exposure to interest rates. If you believe rates will rise, you shorten duration; if you think they'll fall, you extend it.

⌘ Engineer's note

Duration is the first derivative of price with respect to yield — the local slope of that price-yield curve you're dragging above. Notice the curve is convex (it bends), so duration is only a linear approximation; the curvature correction is called convexity, the second derivative. If you've done any numerical sensitivity analysis, this is the same Taylor-expansion logic applied to a bond.

Section 06Credit risk & ratings

So far we assumed the issuer always pays. They don't always. Credit risk (or default risk) is the chance the borrower fails to make payments. Riskier issuers must offer higher yields to attract lenders — that extra yield over a safe government bond is the credit spread.

Rating agencies (CRISIL, ICRA, CARE in India; S&P, Moody's, Fitch globally) grade this risk with letter scores:

Investment gradeAAA → BBBLow default risk; "safe"
High yield / "junk"BB and belowHigher default risk; higher yield

⚠ Ratings are opinions, not guarantees

Agencies are paid by the issuers they rate, and they've been catastrophically wrong before (US mortgage bonds in 2008; IL&FS in India in 2018 was rated AAA shortly before it collapsed). Treat ratings as one input, never gospel. A higher yield is the market telling you there's more risk — it's rarely a free lunch.

Section 07The yield curve

Plot the yields of government bonds against their maturities and you get the yield curve — one of the most-watched pictures in all of finance. Normally it slopes upward: longer bonds pay more, compensating you for tying up money and bearing more uncertainty.

The curve's shape carries a message. A steep curve suggests expected growth and rising rates. A flat curve signals uncertainty. An inverted curve — where short-term yields exceed long-term ones — has preceded almost every modern recession, because it implies the market expects rates (and growth) to fall. You'll meet this signal again in M05's business cycle and M17's banking.

Section 08Types of bonds & the Indian market

  • Government bonds — the safest, backed by the state's taxing power. The benchmark all other bonds are priced against.
  • Corporate bonds — issued by companies; higher yield, more credit risk.
  • Zero-coupon bonds — no coupons; sold at a discount and redeemed at face value (pure M02 lump-sum discounting).
  • Floating-rate bonds — coupons that reset with a reference rate, reducing interest-rate risk.
🇮🇳 India lens

India's government borrows via G-secs (dated securities) and T-bills, auctioned by the RBI, which also manages the government's debt. States issue SDLs (State Development Loans). The 10-year G-sec yield is India's benchmark "risk-free" rate — the anchor for home loans, corporate bonds, and equity valuations alike. Retail investors can now buy G-secs directly through the RBI's Retail Direct platform, and the repo rate from M01 is the lever that moves this entire structure.

★ Why bonds quietly run the world

Equities get the headlines, but the bond market is larger and sets the price of money for everyone. When you understand that bond yields, interest rates, and discount rates are all the same idea wearing different hats, you can see the hidden cable connecting RBI policy → bond yields → loan rates → stock valuations → the whole economy. That cable is the through-line of this entire curriculum.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. A bond is lending: a fixed claim, paid before shareholders, with known returns.
  2. Defined by par, coupon, maturity, issuer; the coupon is fixed but the price moves.
  3. A bond's price = present value of its coupons + face value (pure M02).
  4. Yields and prices move inversely — the single most important fact in fixed income.
  5. Duration measures price sensitivity to yield changes (longer/lower-coupon = more).
  6. Credit risk is default risk; higher risk → higher yield (the credit spread). Ratings are opinions.
  7. The yield curve's shape signals growth expectations; inversion warns of recession.
  8. Government bonds (India: G-secs) set the benchmark risk-free rate for everything else.

Check yourself

Q1 · The RBI raises rates. What happens to the price of bonds you already hold?
Their prices fall. New bonds now offer higher yields, so your older, lower-coupon bonds must drop in price until their yield matches the market. Yields up → prices down, always.
Q2 · Two bonds yield the same, but one has duration 3 and the other duration 12. Which is riskier?
The duration-12 bond. A 1% yield move changes its price by ~12% versus ~3% for the other. Longer duration = far more interest-rate risk — usually from longer maturity and/or lower coupons.
Q3 · A bond's price is below its face value (trading at a discount). What does that tell you about its yield vs its coupon?
Its market yield is above its coupon rate. When yield > coupon, the price sits below par (a discount); when yield < coupon, it trades above par (a premium); when they're equal, it's at par. You can see this live in the pricer above.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Bond
A debt instrument: a loan to an issuer that pays coupons and repays principal at maturity.
Par / face value
The principal repaid at maturity (e.g. ₹1,000).
Coupon
The fixed periodic interest payment, set at issue as a % of face value.
Yield to maturity (YTM)
The discount rate equating a bond's cash flows to its price; the return if held to maturity.
Inverse relationship
Bond prices and yields always move in opposite directions.
Duration
A measure of a bond's price sensitivity to yield changes.
Credit / default risk
The risk the issuer fails to pay; compensated by a credit spread.
Credit rating
An agency's graded opinion of default risk (AAA down to D).
Yield curve
Yields plotted against maturities; its shape signals economic expectations.
G-sec
Government of India dated security — the domestic benchmark "risk-free" bond.

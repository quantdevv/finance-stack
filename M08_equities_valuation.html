Section 01What a share actually is

Strip away the ticker symbols and price charts: a share (or stock, or equity) is a unit of ownership in a company. Own one share of a company that has issued 100 shares, and you own 1% of it — 1% of its assets, 1% of its profits, 1% of the votes.

Crucially, shareholders are residual claimants. When a company earns money, everyone else gets paid first — suppliers, employees, lenders, the taxman. Whatever is left over ("residual") belongs to shareholders. That's the deal: you're last in line, which is why equity is riskier than debt — but the residual can grow without limit, which is why equity can make you rich.

★ The mental model

A bond is a fixed claim (you're owed a set amount). A share is a residual claim (you own whatever's left). That single difference — fixed vs residual — explains almost every difference in risk, return, and behaviour between stocks and bonds. Hold it in mind for all of M08 and M09.

Section 02Common vs preferred & your rights

  • Common (equity) shares — the normal kind. Voting rights, variable dividends, full exposure to upside and downside, last in line on liquidation.
  • Preferred shares — a hybrid: a fixed dividend paid before common shareholders, priority in liquidation, but usually no votes. More bond-like.

As a common shareholder your real powers are modest but meaningful: you can vote on big decisions and the board, you receive dividends if declared, and you can sell your share to anyone at the market price. You do not get to walk into the office and direct the company — that's management's job, overseen by the board you help elect.

Section 03The numbers: market cap, EPS, dividends, buybacks

A handful of figures describe any listed company:

  • Market capitalization = share price × number of shares. The market's total price tag on the whole company. (₹500 × 10 crore shares = ₹5,000 crore.)
  • EPS (earnings per share) = net profit ÷ shares outstanding. The slice of profit attributable to each share — the single most-watched number.
  • Dividend — cash the company pays out to shareholders from profits. Optional; many growth firms pay none and reinvest instead.
  • Buyback — the company buys its own shares back, shrinking the count so each remaining share owns a bigger slice. An alternative way to return cash.
🇮🇳 India lens

Indian companies often have a large promoter holding (founding family / parent group), so "free float" — shares actually available to trade — can be much smaller than total shares. SEBI requires at least 25% public float for listed firms. Watch promoter pledging (shares used as loan collateral): heavy pledging is a classic red flag. Since 2020, dividends are taxed in your hands at your slab rate; buybacks have their own evolving tax treatment.

Section 04Price vs value — the central tension

Here's the idea the entire module orbits. The price is what the market is charging right now — set by supply and demand, mood, momentum, and news. The value (or intrinsic value) is what the business is actually worth based on the cash it will generate. They are not the same number, and the gap between them is where investing happens.

⌘ Engineer's note

Think of price as the observed output of a noisy system and intrinsic value as the true signal you're trying to estimate. Value investing is a bet that price reverts toward value over time. Efficient-market theory (M16, M21) argues the signal and the output are usually already equal. Both camps agree on the framework — they disagree on how much noise there is. You'll meet that debate properly later.

There are two broad ways to estimate value: compare it to others (relative valuation) or compute it from first principles (intrinsic valuation). Every analyst uses both.

Section 05Relative valuation — multiples

The fast, practical approach: judge a stock by comparing a multiple to its peers. The most famous is the P/E ratio:

P/E = Price per share ÷ EPSprice-to-earnings multiple

A P/E of 20 means investors pay ₹20 for every ₹1 of annual earnings — or, flipped, they're implicitly accepting a 5% earnings yield. A high P/E means the market expects strong future growth (or the stock is expensive); a low P/E means low expectations (or a bargain). The number is meaningless alone — it only speaks when compared to peers, history, or the market.

⚠ The trap of multiples

A low P/E is not automatically "cheap." It might be low because the business is dying and the market is right. Comparing multiples only works between genuinely similar companies — same industry, similar growth and risk. A cement company's P/E tells you nothing about a software firm. Multiples are a starting question, not an answer.

Section 06Intrinsic valuation — the dividend discount model

Now the first-principles approach — and here M02 comes roaring back. If a share is worth the present value of all the cash it returns to you, and that cash is dividends growing at a steady rate forever, then it's a growing perpetuity. You already saw the formula. Applied to stocks it's called the Gordon Growth Model:

Value = D1 ÷ (r − g) Gordon growth / dividend discount model

Where D₁ is next year's dividend, r is your required return (the discount rate from M02/M04), and g is the perpetual dividend growth rate. That's it — the value of a whole stock in one line, and it's just the perpetuity you already understand. Play with it:

▸ Notice what the model teaches

As g creeps toward r, the denominator shrinks toward zero and value explodes. This is exactly why a small change in growth assumptions causes huge swings in a stock's "fair value," and why high-growth stocks are so volatile — everyone is arguing about g. It also shows the model's limit: it breaks if g ≥ r (no company grows faster than its discount rate forever). The math is honest about its own boundaries.

Section 07DCF — discounting the whole business

Most companies don't pay steady dividends, so analysts use the more general Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model. The logic is identical to M02's NPV: project the company's free cash flows for several years, discount each back to today, add a terminal value (often a Gordon-growth perpetuity for everything beyond the forecast), and sum it all up.

Value = Σ FCFt ÷ (1+r)t + Terminal value discounted cash flow

DCF is the most rigorous valuation tool and the most dangerous, because its output is exquisitely sensitive to its inputs — the growth rate and the discount rate especially. Change the discount rate by 1% and the "value" can move 20%. This is the famous "garbage in, garbage out" problem: a DCF is only as good as its assumptions, and the assumptions are guesses about the future.

⌘ Engineer's note

A DCF is a model with high sensitivity to its parameters — small input errors amplify into large output errors, exactly like an ill-conditioned system. Smart analysts don't trust a single DCF number; they run a sensitivity table across ranges of r and g and look at the range of plausible values. Treat any single-point valuation with suspicion. The honest output of a DCF is a range, not a number.

Section 08Growth vs value & why this is hard

Two enduring investing styles fall out of all this:

  • Value investing — hunt for stocks trading below intrinsic value; buy the gap and wait for it to close. (Graham, Buffett.)
  • Growth investing — pay up for companies whose earnings will grow fast enough to justify a high price today.

Neither is "right." Both are bets about the future, and the future is unknowable — which is the deep reason valuation is hard. Every model you've met reduces to one honest sentence: a stock is worth the cash it will return, discounted for time and risk — and reasonable people will always disagree about those numbers. That disagreement is the market. You now understand what everyone in M07's room is actually arguing about.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. A share is ownership and a residual claim — last in line, unlimited upside.
  2. Key numbers: market cap, EPS, dividends, buybacks.
  3. Price ≠ value; the gap between them is where investing lives.
  4. Relative valuation compares multiples (P/E, P/B, EV/EBITDA) to peers.
  5. Intrinsic valuation computes value from discounted future cash (M02 returns).
  6. The Gordon Growth Model (D₁/(r−g)) is the perpetuity formula applied to stocks.
  7. DCF is the general tool — rigorous but hypersensitive to assumptions.
  8. Growth vs value are both bets about an unknowable future — hence valuation is hard.

Check yourself

Q1 · A stock's P/E is 10 while its industry trades at 25. Is it cheap?
Maybe — or maybe the market expects its earnings to shrink. A low multiple can signal a bargain or a justified pessimism. You can't tell from the multiple alone; you'd investigate why, and ideally cross-check with an intrinsic valuation.
Q2 · In the Gordon model, why does value rise sharply as g approaches r?
Because value = D₁/(r−g), and as g→r the denominator (r−g) shrinks toward zero, sending value toward infinity. This shows why growth assumptions dominate valuations of fast-growing companies — and why the model breaks down when g ≥ r.
Q3 · Why do professionals distrust a single DCF output number?
Because a DCF is extremely sensitive to its inputs (especially r and g) — tiny changes swing the result enormously. The honest output is a range from a sensitivity analysis, not one precise figure. Garbage in, garbage out.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Share / equity
A unit of ownership in a company; a residual claim on its assets and profits.
Residual claim
Shareholders are paid only after everyone else; they own what's left over.
Market capitalization
Share price × shares outstanding — the market's total value of the company.
EPS
Earnings per share = net profit ÷ shares outstanding.
P/E ratio
Price ÷ EPS — how much investors pay per rupee of earnings.
Intrinsic value
What a business is truly worth based on its future cash flows.
Gordon Growth Model
Value = D₁/(r−g); values a stock as a growing perpetuity of dividends.
DCF
Discounted Cash Flow — value as the sum of discounted future free cash flows plus terminal value.
Buyback
A company repurchasing its own shares, increasing each remaining share's claim.
Free float
Shares actually available to public trading (excludes locked-in promoter holdings).

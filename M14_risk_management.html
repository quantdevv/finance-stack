Section 01What risk actually is

In finance, risk isn't simply "bad things happening" — it's uncertainty about outcomes, the spread of what could happen versus what you expect. Crucially, risk and return are joined at the hip (M11, M13): the expected return is the compensation for bearing risk. You can't earn the one without accepting the other. The goal of risk management isn't to eliminate risk — that would eliminate return — but to take the right risks, in the right size, and avoid the ones that can ruin you.

★ The asymmetry that governs everything

A 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to break even. Lose 90% and you need a 900% gain to recover. Losses and gains are not symmetric — large losses are mathematically far harder to undo than equivalent gains are to keep. This is why "don't lose big" beats "win big": survival compounds, ruin is permanent. The whole discipline flows from this single asymmetry.

Section 02The taxonomy of risk

"Risk" is an umbrella over several distinct dangers. Naming them is the first step to managing them:

TypeWhat it is
Market riskLosses from moves in prices, rates, or FX (the whole market falling)
Credit riskA borrower or counterparty failing to pay (M09)
Liquidity riskNot being able to sell when you need to, except at a bad price (M07)
Operational riskFailures in people, processes, systems, or fraud
Concentration riskToo much exposure to one bet (the opposite of diversification)
Inflation riskYour returns failing to keep pace with rising prices (M01)

Notice that almost every module so far attached to a risk: bonds carry credit and rate risk, illiquid alternatives carry liquidity risk, derivatives carry leverage-amplified market risk. Risk management is the discipline that finally names the bill for every position you hold.

Section 03Measuring risk — volatility

The workhorse measure, from M13, is volatility — the standard deviation of returns. If returns roughly follow a bell curve (normal distribution), volatility tells you how wide that bell is: about two-thirds of outcomes fall within ±1 standard deviation of the mean, and ~95% within ±2.

⌘ Engineer's note

Volatility is just the standard deviation of a time series — the same σ you'd compute for any measurement's noise. The convenient (and dangerous) assumption is that returns are normally distributed, which makes the math clean and gives us tools like VaR. Hold onto the word "dangerous" — Section 06 is about how real markets violate this assumption exactly when it matters most.

Section 04Systematic vs diversifiable risk

A pivotal distinction. Total risk splits into two kinds:

  • Diversifiable (unsystematic) risk — specific to one company or sector: a factory fire, a bad CEO, a product flop. M13's diversification can wash this out by holding many uncorrelated bets.
  • Systematic (market) risk — affects the whole market: recessions, rate shocks, pandemics. You cannot diversify it away, because everything falls together.

This is profound: since diversifiable risk can be removed for free, the market only pays you to bear systematic risk. Taking on company-specific risk you could have diversified away earns you no expected reward — it's uncompensated. A stock's exposure to systematic risk is measured by its beta (β): beta 1 moves with the market, >1 amplifies it, <1 dampens it. Beta is the bridge to the famous CAPM model you'll meet in M21.

Section 05Value at Risk — see your downside

Volatility is symmetric, but what investors really fear is the downside. Value at Risk (VaR) answers a concrete question: "Over a given period, at a given confidence level, what's the most I should expect to lose?" A one-year 95% VaR of ₹50,000 means: in 95% of years you won't lose more than ₹50,000 — but in the worst 5%, you could lose more. Set your portfolio and see the shape of your risk:

Section 06The limits of VaR & tail risk

VaR is useful but seductive, and its limits caused real disasters. Three problems:

  • It says nothing about how bad the tail is. "95% VaR is ₹50k" tells you the threshold, not whether the worst 5% loses ₹60k or ₹6 lakh. The catastrophe lives in the part VaR ignores.
  • Real returns aren't normal. Markets have fat tails — extreme events happen far more often than a bell curve predicts. The 2008 crash was a "25-standard-deviation event" by normal math, which should be impossible. It wasn't.
  • It breeds false confidence. A precise-looking number invites people to forget it's built on shaky assumptions.
⚠ Black swans & the lesson of 2008

The deepest risks are the ones your model says can't happen. Nassim Taleb's "black swan" — a rare, high-impact, retrospectively-rationalised event — is precisely what VaR underweights. The practical wisdom: treat every risk model as a useful fiction, stress-test for scenarios beyond the model, and keep enough margin to survive the event you didn't forecast. Humility is a risk-management tool.

Section 07Hedging & insurance

Beyond diversification, you can actively offset risk. Hedging means taking a position that profits when your main position loses, capping your downside — exactly the options and futures of M10. Buying put options on a stock you own is portfolio insurance; an exporter selling currency forwards locks in the rupee.

▸ Hedging isn't free

Like any insurance, hedging costs a premium that drags on returns in normal times — you pay steadily to be protected in rare bad times. Over-hedging is its own mistake: you can spend so much insuring against downside that you barely earn anything. The art is hedging the risks that could ruin you while accepting the ones you're paid to bear. Insurance against a scratch isn't worth it; insurance against a totalled car is.

Section 08Position sizing & surviving

The most underrated risk tool isn't a formula — it's how much you bet on any one thing. Even a great idea, sized too large, can ruin you if it goes wrong. Professionals cap the loss on any single position (e.g. "never risk more than 1–2% of the portfolio on one trade") so that no single mistake is fatal. This is why the leverage warnings in M10 matter so much: leverage breaks position sizing.

★ The first rule

"Rule No. 1: never lose money. Rule No. 2: never forget Rule No. 1." Buffett's line is really about the loss asymmetry from Section 01. The investor who avoids catastrophic loss stays in the game and lets compounding (M02) do its work over decades. Risk management isn't the opposite of making money — it's the precondition for making it. Defence is what lets the offence compound.

🇮🇳 India lens

For an Indian investor, the most common real-world risks are over-concentration (too much in one's employer stock, one property, or a single sector), inadequate emergency cash (3–6 months of expenses), insufficient insurance (term life and health — genuine risk transfer, separate from investing), and leverage via F&O (M10). Boring, unglamorous risk hygiene — emergency fund, term + health insurance, position limits, diversification — prevents the great majority of personal financial catastrophes.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Risk is uncertainty of outcomes; it's the price of return, not something to eliminate.
  2. Losses and gains are asymmetric — avoiding big losses matters more than chasing big gains.
  3. Risk has types: market, credit, liquidity, operational, concentration, inflation.
  4. Total risk = diversifiable (removable, uncompensated) + systematic (unavoidable, paid for via beta).
  5. VaR estimates a worst expected loss at a confidence level over a period.
  6. VaR has fat-tail blind spots — real markets have black swans models miss.
  7. Hedging offsets risk for a premium; hedge what can ruin you, not every scratch.
  8. Position sizing ensures no single bet is fatal — survival lets compounding work.

Check yourself

Q1 · Why does the market not reward you for taking diversifiable risk?
Because you could have eliminated it for free by diversifying (M13). The market only pays a risk premium for risk that can't be diversified away — systematic risk. Bearing avoidable, company-specific risk adds volatility with no extra expected return.
Q2 · A fund reports a 99% one-day VaR of ₹1 crore. What does this NOT tell you?
It doesn't tell you how bad the worst 1% of days could be — only that on ~99% of days losses stay under ₹1 crore. The size of the disaster in the tail (₹1.2 crore? ₹10 crore?) is exactly what VaR omits, and exactly where ruin lives.
Q3 · Why is avoiding a 50% loss more important than capturing an extra 50% gain?
Because of asymmetry: recovering from a 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to break even, while a missed gain only forgoes upside. Large losses are mathematically much harder to undo, so protecting against them preserves the compounding that builds wealth.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Risk
Uncertainty about outcomes; the spread of possible results around the expected one.
Market / credit / liquidity / operational risk
The major categories of financial danger.
Volatility
Standard deviation of returns; the standard quantitative measure of risk.
Systematic risk
Market-wide risk that can't be diversified away; compensated via the risk premium.
Diversifiable risk
Company/sector-specific risk that diversification removes; uncompensated.
Beta (β)
A measure of a security's sensitivity to overall market moves.
Value at Risk (VaR)
The worst expected loss over a period at a stated confidence level.
Fat tails
Extreme outcomes occurring more often than a normal distribution predicts.
Black swan
A rare, high-impact event that models underweight or miss entirely.
Hedging
Taking an offsetting position to reduce a specific risk, for a cost.

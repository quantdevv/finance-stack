Section 01What a bank actually does

A bank's core business is deceptively simple: it takes in deposits (money it owes you, available on demand) and makes loans (money owed to it, repaid over years). It profits on the gap between the low interest it pays depositors and the higher interest it charges borrowers — the net interest margin.

But notice the magic trick hidden inside: deposits are short-term (you can withdraw anytime) while loans are long-term (a 20-year mortgage). The bank borrows short and lends long. This is maturity transformation, and it's both the source of banking's usefulness and its fragility — the seed of every bank run (Section 08).

★ The function, not the building

A bank's real economic job is channelling savings into investment — exactly the financial-system role from M01, made concrete. It pools millions of small, short-term deposits and transforms them into the large, long-term loans that build houses, factories, and businesses. Without this transformation, savers and borrowers could never find each other at the right size and term. The bank is the system's matchmaker.

Section 02The bank's balance sheet

Apply M03's accounting equation to a bank and something surprising appears. To a bank, your deposit is a liability (it owes you), and the loans it makes are assets (others owe it):

Assets (what it owns)Liabilities & equity (what it owes)
Loans to customersCustomer deposits
Reserves (cash at central bank)Borrowings
Government bonds (G-secs)Shareholders' equity (capital)

A bank holds only a thin sliver of equity (capital) relative to its huge assets — it's extraordinarily leveraged (M06). That capital is the cushion that absorbs losses when loans go bad. Too thin a cushion and a wave of defaults wipes out the bank — which is why regulators obsess over capital adequacy.

Section 03Fractional reserves & money creation

Here's the idea that genuinely surprises most people. Banks don't keep all your deposits in the vault — they keep only a fraction as reserves and lend the rest out. This is fractional-reserve banking, and its consequence is profound: the banking system creates money.

When a bank lends out your deposit, that money gets spent and re-deposited in another bank, which keeps a fraction and lends the rest again... and so on. Each round creates new deposits — new money — out of the original. The total money created from an initial deposit is governed by the money multiplier:

Money multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratioa 10% reserve ratio multiplies money 10×
⌘ Engineer's note

This is a geometric series: D + D(1−r) + D(1−r)² + ... = D/r. The same convergent-sum math behind the perpetuity (M02) drives money creation. Most of the "money" in an economy (the M1/M2 of M01) isn't printed by the government — it's ledger entries created by commercial banks lending. The central bank prints only the base; the banking system multiplies it. Mind-bending, and entirely real.

⚠ A power and a danger

Money creation fuels growth — credit lets the economy build more than its existing savings alone could fund. But unchecked, it inflates bubbles (M16) and, when loans sour en masse, the process runs in reverse: lending contracts, money is destroyed, and you get a credit crunch and recession (2008). The central bank's job is to keep this engine running at the right speed — not too cold, not too hot.

Section 05Commercial vs investment banking

"Bank" covers two very different businesses:

  • Commercial (retail) banking — the deposits-and-loans business above. Savings accounts, mortgages, business loans, payments. This is HDFC Bank, SBI, ICICI in their everyday role.
  • Investment banking — serves companies and institutions, not the public: helping firms raise capital (managing IPOs and bond issues — the primary market of M07), advising on mergers & acquisitions (M06), and trading securities. Goldman Sachs, and the investment-banking arms of big banks.

The 2008 crisis was partly blamed on the blurring of these lines — deposit-taking banks taking investment-banking-style risks with depositors' money. Many countries have since pushed to wall them apart again, so that ordinary deposits aren't gambling chips.

Section 06The central bank — the RBI

Above all the commercial banks sits the central bank — in India, the Reserve Bank of India. It's not a bank for you; it's the bank for banks and the government, with a unique set of jobs:

  • Monetary policy — managing interest rates and money supply to control inflation and support growth (M01, M05).
  • Issuing currency — it prints the base money.
  • Banker to banks & government — holds their reserves, manages government debt (the G-sec auctions of M09).
  • Lender of last resort — lends emergency cash to solvent banks facing a run, stopping panic from spreading.
  • Regulator & supervisor — keeps banks safe and sound.

Section 07Monetary policy tools

How does the RBI actually steer the economy? Through levers that change how much money costs and how much exists:

ToolWhat it does
Repo rateThe rate RBI lends to banks; the master interest-rate lever (M01)
CRRCash Reserve Ratio — % of deposits banks must park at RBI
SLRStatutory Liquidity Ratio — % held in safe assets like G-secs
OMOOpen Market Operations — buying/selling G-secs to add or drain cash

Raising the repo rate makes borrowing costlier, cooling demand and inflation; cutting it stimulates. Raising CRR/SLR shrinks how much banks can lend (lowering the money multiplier you just played with). These tools all ultimately push on the same thing: the price and quantity of money, which ripples out to every loan, bond, and stock valuation in the country.

🇮🇳 India lens

The RBI's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets bi-monthly to set the repo rate, with an official mandate to keep inflation at 4% (±2%) (M01). When you read "RBI holds rates" or "RBI hikes by 25 bps," this is the machinery in action — and it's why every Indian borrower, saver, and investor watches these announcements. The repo rate is the single most important number in Indian finance.

Section 08Bank runs, safety & regulation

Recall the fragility from Section 01: a bank can't return all deposits at once because they're lent out long-term. If enough depositors demand their money simultaneously — a bank run — even a healthy bank can collapse, because the cash simply isn't in the vault. Worse, runs are self-fulfilling: the fear of a run causes the run.

Society defends against this with layers of protection: deposit insurance (in India, the DICGC guarantees deposits up to ₹5 lakh per bank), the central bank as lender of last resort, and strict capital and liquidity rules (the international Basel standards) ensuring banks hold enough cushion. These exist because a banking collapse doesn't stay contained — it freezes credit for the whole economy.

★ Why banks are special

Banking is the one industry where one firm's failure can topple the others — deposits, loans, and payments are all interconnected, so a single big collapse can cascade (systemic risk, M14). That's why banks are the most heavily regulated businesses on earth, why "too big to fail" became a phrase, and why central banks exist at all. The whole apparatus of M17 is society's answer to one fragile, indispensable trick: borrowing short to lend long.

The eight things to carry forward

  1. Banks take short-term deposits and make long-term loans — maturity transformation.
  2. On a bank's books, your deposit is a liability and its loans are assets; it runs on thin capital.
  3. Fractional-reserve banking means banks lend out most deposits, creating money.
  4. The money multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratio; most money is bank-created ledger entries.
  5. Commercial banking (deposits/loans) differs from investment banking (capital-raising, M&A).
  6. The central bank (RBI) is the bank for banks: monetary policy, currency, lender of last resort.
  7. Policy tools — repo rate, CRR, SLR, OMO — push on the price and quantity of money.
  8. Bank runs threaten the system; deposit insurance, Basel capital rules, and the RBI guard against them.

Check yourself

Q1 · Why is your bank deposit a liability for the bank, not an asset?
Because the bank owes that money back to you on demand — it's a claim you hold against the bank. The bank's assets are the loans it has made (money others owe it) and its reserves. This flip is the key to reading a bank's balance sheet correctly (M03).
Q2 · With a 20% reserve ratio, how much total money can ₹10,000 of new deposits ultimately create?
₹50,000. The money multiplier is 1 ÷ 0.20 = 5, so ₹10,000 × 5 = ₹50,000 of total deposits across the system (of which ₹40,000 is newly created via successive rounds of lending). Lower the reserve ratio and the multiplier — and the money created — rises.
Q3 · Why can even a healthy bank be destroyed by a bank run?
Because of maturity transformation: deposits are repayable on demand but the bank's money is tied up in long-term loans that can't be called back instantly. If too many depositors demand cash at once, the bank can't produce it — not because it's insolvent, but because it's illiquid. Deposit insurance and the lender-of-last-resort exist to stop this fear-driven spiral.

Glossary — terms to know cold

Maturity transformation
Funding long-term loans with short-term deposits — banking's core trick and risk.
Net interest margin
The spread between interest earned on loans and paid on deposits.
Fractional-reserve banking
Holding only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lending the rest.
Money multiplier
1 ÷ reserve ratio; how much total money an initial deposit can create.
Capital adequacy
The requirement that banks hold enough equity to absorb losses (Basel rules).
Commercial vs investment bank
Deposits-and-loans business vs capital-raising/M&A/trading business.
Central bank (RBI)
The bank for banks and government; sets monetary policy and is lender of last resort.
Repo rate
The rate at which the central bank lends to banks — the master interest-rate lever.
CRR / SLR
Cash Reserve Ratio / Statutory Liquidity Ratio — required reserves and safe-asset holdings.
Bank run
A simultaneous rush of depositors to withdraw, which can collapse a solvent bank.
